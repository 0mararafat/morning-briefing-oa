Iran Mines Hormuz, Halts Talks — Oil Surges Back Toward $100

Iran has mined large segments of the Strait of Hormuz and suspended US negotiations, threatening to fully close the chokepoint and triggering the largest energy supply disruption in history.
▾

The 2026 Iran war, which began on February 28 when the US and Israel launched nearly 900 strikes targeting Iranian military infrastructure and killed Supreme Leader Ali Khamenei, has entered a dangerous new phase. Secretary of State Rubio told the Senate Foreign Relations Committee on June 2 that Iran has mined 'large segments' of Hormuz — international waters — and is firing on commercial ships. Ship traffic through the strait, which handled roughly 20% of the world's seaborne oil before the war, has largely collapsed. Iran's state-affiliated Tasnim News reported Monday that Iranian negotiators will stop exchanging messages with the US through intermediaries and will move to fully close the Strait of Hormuz in retaliation for ongoing ceasefire violations, specifically citing Israeli military operations in Lebanon against Hezbollah.

Oil prices climbed back toward $100 per barrel on June 3, halting the US stock market's record-breaking rally. The disruption has been described by analysts at Barclays and Goldman Sachs as the largest supply disruption in the history of the global oil market. Europe faces acute energy stress: the conflict has precipitated a second major energy crisis, with Dutch TTF gas benchmarks having nearly doubled to over €60/MWh by mid-March. The ECB has warned a prolonged conflict will likely trigger stagflation and push Germany and Italy into technical recession by year-end.

The House approved a war powers resolution on June 3 to halt military action against Iran, in a rebuke of Trump. The vote is largely symbolic given the Senate and White House, but signals the depth of congressional restiveness. Kuwait reported Iranian drone strikes hitting its airport and killing one person as recently as June 3, testing the fragile April 7-8 ceasefire.

The path to reopening the strait is arduous even if a deal is struck. Analysts at S&P Global and Lloyd's List note that roughly 150 large merchant ships transited the strait daily before the war; Iran has pledged to facilitate a return to prewar shipping levels within 30 days of any agreement, but demining alone could take months. Key watch: whether Trump accepts any Iran-Oman power-sharing arrangement over the strait — which he publicly rejected on May 28.

HIGH AI Regulation & Governance

Trump Signs AI Executive Order: 30-Day Pre-Release Window for Frontier Models

Trump quietly signed an executive order June 2 asking AI labs on a voluntary basis to give the government 30-day early access to frontier models to assess their cybersecurity capabilities — explicitly stopping short of mandatory licensing.
▾

President Trump signed the 'Promoting Advanced Artificial Intelligence Innovation and Security' executive order on June 2, without a signing ceremony and after postponing a planned event with tech CEOs. The order establishes a classified benchmarking process, run by the NSA in consultation with CISA, NIST, and the National Cyber Director, to identify models powerful enough to be designated 'covered frontier models.' It then invites AI developers, on a purely voluntary basis, to provide early access to those models up to 30 days before broader release, so the government can assess their 'advanced cyber capabilities.' The order explicitly states that nothing shall be construed to authorize mandatory licensing, pre-clearance, or permitting for AI model development or distribution.

The trigger for the order was Anthropic's Claude Mythos Preview model, which demonstrated superhuman ability to find critical vulnerabilities in widely-used operating systems, sending — according to NBC News — 'shockwaves' through Washington. The order also directs CISA to issue Binding Operational Directives to expand AI-enabled cyber defense across civilian federal systems, and creates an AI cybersecurity clearinghouse for vulnerability scanning and patch distribution.

The order's voluntary nature is its defining political characteristic. CNBC reported that Musk, Zuckerberg, and David Sacks called the Trump administration last month to lobby against an earlier, more prescriptive version of the order. The current text is a compromise: Washington gets a private preview window and a governance framework without imposing the kind of mandatory pre-release review that Silicon Valley feared. Whether labs actually participate voluntarily — especially Anthropic, which is locked in litigation with the administration — is the key question to watch.

The Anthropic v. DOD lawsuit remains on a parallel track. The D.C. Circuit declined to stay the supply chain risk designation in April. A San Francisco federal court did grant Anthropic a preliminary injunction barring the Trump administration from enforcing a Claude usage ban. Both cases are ongoing with expedited scheduling.

Google DeepMind Drops Gemma 4 12B: Frontier AI for Laptops

Google DeepMind released Gemma 4 12B, an open-source multimodal model designed to run on consumer hardware with 16GB of VRAM, targeting the gap between cloud-only frontier models and heavily compressed local deployments.
▾

Google DeepMind on Wednesday released Gemma 4 12B, a new open-source multimodal model delivering advanced reasoning, coding, vision, and audio capabilities on hardware many developers already own. The model is optimized for laptops and desktops with roughly 16GB of VRAM or unified memory — covering modern Windows machines and Apple's MacBook lineup. It supports a 256,000-token context window, sufficient for large codebases, lengthy documents, and agentic workflows requiring extended reasoning.

Benchmark results are competitive: Gemma 4 12B posted 77.5% on AIME 2026 and 79.7% on MATH-Vision for mathematical reasoning, 94.9% on DocVQA and 88.4% on InfoVQA for document understanding. Google's pitch is access: large multimodal models typically demand cloud infrastructure or heavily quantized local deployments, and Gemma 4 12B targets a middle ground that reduces inference costs and latency for edge developers.

The release fits a broader industry trend of frontier capability democratization. At Computex last month, Nvidia unveiled the RTX Spark Superchip — 20 Arm cores, a Blackwell GPU, and 128GB of unified memory in a Windows laptop Nvidia says can run a 120-billion-parameter model with a million-token context entirely on-device, with over 30 laptops and 10 desktops shipping this fall. At Microsoft Build 2026, the company unveiled Project Polaris, an in-house coding model running on Maia chips, replacing GPT-4 Turbo as the GitHub Copilot default. The on-device AI arms race is accelerating; watch how Gemma 4 12B adoption signals developer migration away from API-dependent workflows.

The Safety Lab Goes Public: Anthropic's IPO Forces a Reckoning Over Whether AI Responsibility Can Survive Wall Street

Anthropic's confidential SEC filing this week, arriving alongside Trump's voluntary AI oversight order, poses the defining governance question of the AI era: can a mission to build safe AI survive the incentive structures of public capitalism?
Anthropic's confidential S-1 filing was confirmed June 1 and Trump's AI executive order was signed June 2, placing the foundational tension between AI safety governance and commercial scale at the center of this week's news cycle.

Three events landed in the same 72-hour window this week, and taken together they constitute something close to a stress test for the entire premise of responsible AI development. On June 1, Anthropic confirmed it had confidentially submitted a draft S-1 registration statement to the SEC, formally opening the path to a public listing. On June 2, President Trump signed an executive order establishing a voluntary framework for pre-release government review of frontier AI models. And hovering above both developments, a new Harvard Law paper landed with uncomfortably precise timing, arguing that Anthropic's governance structure — designed to place a safety mission above short-term shareholder interests — may not survive serious pressure from public markets.

The financial facts are extraordinary. Anthropic closed a $65 billion Series H round just days before the filing, pushing its post-money valuation to $965 billion — briefly overtaking OpenAI's $852 billion private-market figure. The company's annualized revenue run rate crossed $47 billion in May, up from roughly $10 billion the prior year, driven largely by Claude Code, its enterprise coding assistant. Analysts now widely expect a public debut above $1 trillion, which would place Anthropic among the world's fifteen most valuable companies on day one. The company is targeting an October 2026 window, though timeline depends on SEC review and market conditions.

The governance wrinkle is the thing that makes this IPO structurally different from any other tech listing in recent memory. Anthropic is a Public Benefit Corporation, meaning its board is legally required to balance financial returns against broader societal benefit — including its stated mission to develop AI that is "safe, interpretable, and aligned with human values." Paired with that is a Long-Term Benefit Trust, designed to insulate safety commitments from investor pressure. The Harvard Law paper, co-authored by professor Jesse Fried, offers a sobering verdict on such structures: of the three companies ever to install investor-overriding mission guardians in a for-profit context, one ended in spectacular failure and one has already experienced serious governance breakdown. The third is Anthropic. The paper notes that a supermajority of investors can, in theory, terminate the Trust entirely — a kill switch that may render the safety architecture moot under real pressure, even if it also disciplines the guardians from going too far.

Public markets will ask questions that private backers never needed answered. How will analysts value safety investments that reduce near-term margins? What happens when the pressure to train larger models faster conflicts with the company's responsible-scaling policy? Anthropic's own history with the Pentagon is instructive: earlier this year, a contract was effectively terminated after the company declined to relax its restrictions on autonomous weapons use, and the Pentagon moved to designate it a supply-chain risk. That dispute, which Anthropic is contesting, previews how the safety mission can generate real commercial cost — cost that public shareholders will watch closely.

The Trump executive order adds a second, intersecting layer of complexity. Signed June 2 after a near-miss when Trump pulled back a May 21 signing over fears the order would stifle American competitiveness against China, the final version asks AI companies to voluntarily submit their most powerful models for government review up to 30 days before public release. The order explicitly bars mandatory licensing or permitting, and the framework gives the NSA discretion to set classified benchmarks determining which models qualify as "covered frontier" systems. For Anthropic, the practical implication is notable: the government would also have a role in selecting which companies get early trusted-partner access to frontier models — without published criteria for those selections.

The reasonable disagreements here run deep. Optimists argue the voluntary framework is pragmatically superior to the EU's mandatory risk-classification regime, which critics say risks locking in today's capability thresholds as permanent regulatory lines. They also contend that Anthropic's governance design, while imperfect, is the most serious attempt yet to institutionalize safety accountability in a high-growth company, and that public scrutiny may actually strengthen rather than weaken that commitment. Skeptics counter that the voluntary executive order has no enforcement mechanism — "any regulations around the technology would have to come from Congress," as NPR noted — making it a statement of aspiration rather than a constraint. On the Anthropic side, they argue that a company valued at nearly a trillion dollars faces an asymmetry: the safety structures that protect the mission cost something, while the competitive pressures to move faster, expand into defense, and hit quarterly profit targets do not relent.

The timeline over the next three months will be decisive on multiple fronts. Within 60 days, federal agencies must design the voluntary pre-release framework and begin building the AI cybersecurity clearinghouse. SpaceX, the first of the big three tech listings, is reportedly beginning its roadshow around June 4; its reception will set a valuation comparable against which both Anthropic and OpenAI's subsequent offerings will be measured. Anthropic's public S-1, when it is released, will include the most closely-watched governance disclosure in AI history — specifically how the Long-Term Benefit Trust interacts with public shareholder rights. If that disclosure is robust and investors price it as a feature rather than a bug, it could establish a durable template. If the market dismisses it as window dressing, the precedent set will reverberate across every safety-focused AI lab still in private hands.
