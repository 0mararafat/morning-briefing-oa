House Passes War Powers Rebuke on Iran — But Trump Is Unmoved

A bipartisan House majority voted 215-208 to curb Trump's war powers in Iran, the clearest congressional revolt yet, while US-Iran strikes continued the same day and ceasefire talks are stalled.
▾

In a historically significant vote late Wednesday, the House passed a war powers resolution 215-208, with four Republicans joining all Democrats to direct the president to end hostilities with Iran. The passage marks the fourth such attempt this year — but the first to actually clear the chamber. As NPR and Al Jazeera reported, the resolution still requires Senate approval, where a similar measure has not yet received a final vote, and even passage would face a near-certain presidential veto. It is largely symbolic in legal force, but its political weight is enormous: it signals that Trump's grip on his own party is cracking over a war now in its 97th day.

The war itself remains unresolved and dangerous. According to AP and Britannica's real-time tracker, Iranian drones struck Kuwait's airport on June 3, killing one person, as the conditional ceasefire declared April 8 continues to fray. Trump claimed this morning he is 'boxed in,' per CNN analysis. Pentagon, State Department, and USAID inspectors general announced a joint review of the war, citing statutory obligations that kick in when hostilities exceed 60 days.

The war's economic toll is front-page material. The Strait of Hormuz, through which 20% of global petroleum and 20% of LNG normally flows, remains effectively closed — shipping traffic is around 5% of pre-war levels per the UK House of Commons Library. The IEA has called it the 'greatest global energy security challenge in history.' Oil prices are climbing back toward $100/barrel as of June 3 (AP). The US consumer is feeling it: gas prices have risen over $1/gallon since February, diesel is spiking, and new Fed chair Kevin Warsh faces an economy under oil-shock strain at his first FOMC meeting June 16-17.

Speaker Mike Johnson warned the resolution 'weakens negotiations' while Rep. Thomas Massie (R-KY) said his constituents are 'tired of $5-gallon gas.' Midterm election calculations are clearly reshaping GOP attitudes. Democrats are urging the Senate to act fast. The watch item: whether Senate Republicans continue peeling away, and whether Pakistan-mediated nuclear-adjacent negotiations produce a more durable peace framework.

Trump Signs AI Security Executive Order — Voluntary Frontier Model Access, No Mandates

Signed June 2, the order creates a classified benchmarking process for 'covered frontier models' and asks AI companies to voluntarily share models 30 days pre-release — but explicitly bans mandatory licensing or preclearance.
▾

On June 2, Trump signed the executive order 'Promoting Advanced Artificial Intelligence Innovation and Security,' the most substantive federal AI policy action since he revoked the Biden-era order in January 2025. The order directs NSA, CISA, Treasury, and NIST to build a classified benchmarking process to assess AI models' 'advanced cyber capabilities,' designate which qualify as 'covered frontier models,' and establish an AI cybersecurity clearinghouse. Under the voluntary framework, AI companies may submit covered frontier models for government review up to 30 days before public release, allowing the government to identify security vulnerabilities and choose 'trusted partners' for early access.

Critically, the order is emphatic that it does not create 'a mandatory governmental licensing, preclearance, or permitting requirement' for AI models — a deliberate contrast to EU-style regulation. AI companies are partners, not regulated entities, per the order's framing. CNBC reported Trump signed it privately, weeks after postponing a signing ceremony when Musk, Zuckerberg, and David Sacks lobbied against an earlier draft they felt threatened US competitiveness.

The order's 60-day timeline creates concrete deliverables by early August 2026: the classified benchmarking framework must be operational, the clearinghouse stood up, and CISA must issue binding operational directives on AI-enabled cyber defense. WilmerHale and Crowell & Moring both noted in client alerts that while voluntary today, the framework 'may well migrate into procurement standards, sectoral cybersecurity guidance, and contractual requirements over time' — particularly for defense contractors and critical infrastructure operators.

The backdrop matters: Anthropic's Claude Mythos Preview, which excels at software vulnerability discovery, and OpenAI's GPT-5.5 prompted the Institute for AI Policy and Strategy to call for urgent frontier model governance in May. Separately, the DoD has designated Anthropic a 'supply chain risk,' barring defense contractors from using its models — litigation over that designation is ongoing. The Colorado AI Act, due to take effect June 30, remains a flashpoint; the White House's AI Litigation Task Force has targeted it as 'onerous.' Safety advocates argue the new EO doesn't go nearly far enough, with no mandatory pre-deployment testing or binding safety standards.

The West Splits on AI: Washington Bets on Voluntarism While Brussels Moves to Shut Out American Cloud

Within 24 hours this week, the United States and the European Union unveiled diametrically opposed AI governance visions — one built on light-touch deference to industry, the other on asserting digital independence from American and Asian tech giants.
President Trump's AI and cybersecurity executive order was signed Monday June 2 and the EU's Tech Sovereignty Package was unveiled Tuesday June 3, making this week the most consequential 48-hour stretch in AI governance since the EU AI Act's general-purpose AI provisions came into force.

On June 2, President Trump signed an executive order titled 'Promoting Advanced Artificial Intelligence Innovation and Security,' and just one day later, the European Commission unveiled its sweeping European Technological Sovereignty Package. The near-simultaneous release of these two major policy documents is not coincidental symmetry — it is the clearest expression yet of a fundamental transatlantic rift over who controls the future of artificial intelligence and on whose terms.

The Trump order reflects a philosophy that has defined the administration's AI posture since day one: deregulate, accelerate, and keep government out of the way. Its centerpiece is a voluntary framework asking AI developers to submit 'covered frontier models' to federal agencies for a 30-day pre-release review focused on cybersecurity vulnerabilities. Critically, the order explicitly prohibits the creation of any mandatory licensing or preclearance requirement. The review period was itself the subject of intense last-minute lobbying: an earlier draft had proposed a 90-day window, before tech allies including prominent venture capitalists pushed back, arguing that in the AI race, every week of delay matters. The final 30-day window is voluntary throughout. The order also establishes an AI cybersecurity clearinghouse under the Treasury Department, working with the NSA and CISA, to coordinate vulnerability scanning across the private sector.

The EU package, released the following morning in Brussels, reads like a strategic rebuttal — even if its drafting long predates this week's American order. The Commission proposed four interlocking measures: a Chips Act 2.0 aimed at building European semiconductor capacity for AI; a Cloud and AI Development Act (CADA) that would create a four-tier sovereignty framework for cloud services and effectively restrict member governments from using U.S. providers to process sensitive public-sector data; an EU Open Source Strategy; and an energy-AI digitalisation roadmap. The CADA would require public authorities to conduct sovereignty risk assessments for cloud and AI procurement across healthcare, finance, and judicial systems — a direct structural challenge to the dominance of Amazon, Microsoft, and Google in European government infrastructure. The urgency behind the package is partly political: fears of a 'kill switch' embedded in U.S. commercial platforms crystallised when Microsoft terminated the email account of the International Criminal Court's chief prosecutor after American sanctions, demonstrating in vivid terms what dependency can mean in practice.

The deeper context is a decade of European frustration. The EU currently relies on non-EU countries for over 80 percent of key digital products and services, and produces under 10 percent of the world's semiconductors. The 2023 Chips Act committed €52 billion toward doubling Europe's global chip market share to 20 percent by 2030 — a target that now looks difficult to reach. The CADA aims to triple EU data-centre capacity within five to seven years, channelling an estimated €200 billion in public and private investment. Whether the capital will materialise is the central open question: Brussels itself acknowledged that Europe lacks the risk capital needed to scale domestic tech champions.

The competing interpretations here are sharp and genuinely contested. Proponents of the Trump approach argue that voluntary frameworks, rather than mandatory pre-clearance, preserve America's structural advantage in frontier AI development — the very speed of iteration that has kept U.S. labs ahead of Chinese competitors. Critics note that a purely voluntary system offers no enforceable guarantee that a dangerous model won't be released: the order has no mechanism to stop a launch, only to observe it in advance. On the EU side, supporters argue that sovereignty is a precondition for trust in AI systems that govern hospitals, courts, and energy grids. Sceptics — including some of the package's own technical advisers — warn that a 'full retreat into a Europe-first tech approach will leave the continent weaker,' in the words of the Tony Blair Institute's director of science and technology, and that without global deployment ambition, European AI will simply fall further behind.

There is also a China dimension that neither bloc is addressing directly. China's 15th Five-Year Plan, now in full implementation, places AI, robotics, quantum, and 6G at the centre of its industrial strategy, with state backing designed to accelerate these sectors to commercial viability. Chinese AI models are increasingly high-quality, cost-efficient, and open source — a combination that appeals to governments across the Global South. As the West fragments into two governance regimes, Beijing's offer of a third path — practical, cheap, and ideologically uncomplicated — gains relative attractiveness.

Watch, over the coming months, for three specific developments. First, how many EU member states push back on the CADA sovereignty tiers during the legislative negotiation process — the package still requires approval from all 27 members and the European Parliament, a process likely to take years. Second, whether major US AI labs — starting with Anthropic, whose Claude Mythos model directly prompted the executive order — formally participate in the new 30-day voluntary review regime, or treat it as a bureaucratic formality. Third, and perhaps most consequentially, whether the Colorado AI Act's enforcement deadline of June 30 triggers the federal preemption litigation that the Trump administration's Department of Justice has been preparing since January — a confrontation that would clarify once and for all whether states retain any meaningful power to regulate AI within American borders.
