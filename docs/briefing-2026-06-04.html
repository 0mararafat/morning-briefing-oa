URGENT AI Regulation & Governance

Trump Signs AI Executive Order: Government Gets 30-Day Early Access to Frontier Models

A new White House executive order — signed June 2 — creates a voluntary classified benchmarking process for frontier AI models and asks companies to give the government pre-release access, while pointedly barring any mandatory licensing regime.
▾

President Trump on June 2 signed an executive order titled 'Promoting Advanced Artificial Intelligence Innovation and Security,' directing agencies to build a classified benchmarking framework to assess whether AI models qualify as 'covered frontier models' based on their advanced cyber capabilities. Companies are invited, on a voluntary basis, to provide the government access to those models up to 30 days before public release. The order explicitly states that nothing in it creates a mandatory licensing, preclearance, or permitting requirement — a deliberate signal to the industry that the administration is not following the EU's more prescriptive path.

The order assigns responsibility to a cross-agency team including NSA, CISA, NIST, Treasury, and the National Cyber Director, with a 60-day deadline — meaning deliverables are due by approximately August 1, 2026. It also directs the Treasury to form an AI cybersecurity clearinghouse for identifying and remediating software vulnerabilities, and instructs the Attorney General to prioritise enforcement of computer fraud statutes against those who weaponise AI for cybercrime.

CNBC reported that the order arrived weeks after Trump had postponed a signing ceremony because he 'didn't like certain aspects of it,' citing reported lobbying by David Sacks, Elon Musk, and Mark Zuckerberg against an earlier draft. The final text is notably thinner on detail than that prior version. A separate and ongoing storyline: the DOD has designated Anthropic a 'supply chain risk,' barring defence contractors from using Claude; Anthropic has sued to reverse this designation and that litigation continues.

Policy observers note this order builds on Trump's broader 2025-2026 AI governance sequence — a July 2025 AI Action Plan, a December 2025 state-preemption EO, a March 2026 National Cyber Strategy, and a March 2026 National AI Policy Framework. The Colorado AI Act, due to take effect June 30, remains a live flashpoint, with the administration having previously targeted it by name as an obstacle to innovation.

HIGH China

China Tightens Outbound Investment Controls on AI, Bans Tech Workers from Leaving

Beijing's June 1 State Council rules tighten control over outbound investment in AI and national security sectors, adding to a growing toolkit of economic coercion that now includes exit bans on AI engineers.
▾

On June 1, China's State Council published new regulations tightening control over outbound investment in key sectors including AI and national security-related technologies. The rules allow Beijing to review planned deals touching national security, ban cross-border data and technology transfers, and — critically — restrict Chinese engineers from leaving the country. This follows a precedent set in March when China barred the cofounders of AI startup Manus from departing after the company's rumoured exit to Singapore.

The Foundation for Defense of Democracies, which first reported the rules in detail, notes these regulations are part of Beijing's broader effort to consolidate economic coercion tools — including export controls, sanctions, and exit bans — to exercise domestic economic control while hindering US pressure. They follow earlier April 2026 supply chain regulations that may prevent firms from exiting contracts with Chinese suppliers, and laws banning AI workers from leaving the country. Under the new authorities, Beijing may cut off trade ties, revoke visas, cancel market access agreements, or impose sanctions against entities it deems threats to national sovereignty.

The move comes against a backdrop of a fragile US-China trade truce following the May Trump-Xi summit in Beijing, with CFR noting that high tariffs, rare earth restrictions, and tech export controls remain major sticking points. China controls 85-90% of global rare earth processing capacity, giving it continued leverage at critical input choke points for semiconductors and AI hardware.

Watchers should monitor how the US responds to this latest regulatory escalation, particularly as Sam Altman is scheduled to meet with lawmakers and Trump officials in Washington this week — a visit that may shape the administration's posture on China tech decoupling.

The political sensitivity is acute. Warsh and Trump used the swearing-in ceremony to jointly reaffirm Fed independence — a pledge that CFR notes 'will be tested early' given the president's explicit rate-cut expectations.

TODAY'S DEEP DIVE

Trump Signed an AI Safety Order — Then Hid the Pen

A quietly signed executive order demanding only voluntary pre-deployment reviews of frontier AI models reveals the profound contradiction at the heart of American AI governance: invoking national security while refusing to impose the obligations security demands.
The executive order was signed Tuesday and its legal architecture was only fully published Wednesday night, with the first detailed independent legal analysis appearing this morning — making today the moment when the policy community is reckoning with what it actually does and does not require.

On the morning of June 2, President Trump signed an executive order titled "Promoting Advanced Artificial Intelligence Innovation and Security" — without cameras, without ceremony, and without the Rose Garden theatrics he typically reserves for marquee executive actions. The quiet signing was itself a tell. The order asks AI companies to voluntarily submit their most powerful frontier models to the federal government for review up to 30 days before public release. It also directs agencies to create an "AI cybersecurity clearinghouse" to coordinate vulnerability scanning, and tasks CISA with hardening civilian federal systems against AI-enabled threats. On paper, it is the Trump administration's most substantive engagement with AI safety since taking office. In practice, critics argue it amounts to regulatory performance art.

The backstory matters as much as the text. An earlier draft of the same order had set a 90-day pre-deployment window — mandatory, not optional. That version was nearly signed on May 21, before Trump publicly pulled back, saying he didn't support "certain aspects of it" and worried it would stifle domestic AI development. The architect of that reversal was reportedly David Sacks, the former "AI czar," who convinced the president that anything resembling mandatory review would hand Beijing a competitive advantage. The final order's 30-day voluntary window is the residue of that internal battle — a compromise between a national security faction that sees unchecked frontier models as a genuine cyber threat, and a deregulatory faction that treats any oversight mechanism as anathema to American innovation.

The order's structural novelty lies in a provision allowing developers who voluntarily participate to collaborate with the federal government on selecting "trusted partners" to receive early model access. A law firm analysis published the same day noted that the order provides no criteria whatsoever for how those trusted partners are chosen — giving the government a potentially sweeping, standardless role in determining who gets first access to the world's most capable AI systems, with no transparency or accountability mechanism attached.

The reaction has fractured along predictable but revealing lines. Senator Josh Hawley praised the order as a safety step, but immediately called for his own bipartisan legislation — co-sponsored with Senators Blumenthal and Blackburn — that would make government review of advanced AI systems mandatory, not optional. The Alliance for Secure AI was blunter: voluntary reviews are insufficient, and Congress must codify the framework with enforceable requirements. Meanwhile, the order landed in the same week that a Republican lawmaker called for legal action against nonprofits he alleged are backed by China to seed opposition to the AI buildout — a narrative that Semafor reported on Thursday has been amplifying in conservative circles, with tech figures and the Trump administration invoking Beijing as the force behind grassroots resistance to data centers. Experts pushed back sharply, with US-China tech scholar Samm Sacks telling Semafor that Chinese state media is more likely exploiting existing American divisions than manufacturing them from scratch.

That Beijing angle is not mere distraction — it is load-bearing to the order's political logic. The administration's argument for keeping review voluntary rather than mandatory is explicitly framed around competition with China. But this creates a paradox: if Chinese AI development is the existential threat that justifies the entire national security framing, then accepting weaker oversight to avoid slowing American labs down is not a winning posture — it simply relocates the vulnerability. Critics note that a voluntary regime means the most reckless developers, by definition, have no incentive to participate. The companies most likely to submit models are precisely those already engaged with the government — Anthropic, Microsoft, OpenAI — which raises the question of what, exactly, the order adds beyond formalising relationships that already exist informally.

The deeper issue is institutional. The order tasks Treasury, NSA, and CISA with building this framework within 60 days, but gives them no new legal authority, no budget line, and no mandate to compel participation. The cybersecurity clearinghouse is also voluntary. Analysts familiar with previous voluntary coordination efforts — from the Biden-era AI safety commitments to sector-specific ISAC models — note that such bodies tend to produce value in good-faith environments but collapse precisely when the actors with the most dangerous capabilities feel least incentive to share.

The next 90 days will be determinative. Congress now faces pressure from multiple directions: Hawley's mandatory review bill, the Sanders-AOC data center moratorium (which would go in the opposite direction entirely), and the first major AI IPO filings from Anthropic and OpenAI that will bring frontier AI governance into public market disclosure requirements for the first time. If the administration's voluntary framework is seen to be producing substantive government access to capable models — and if no major AI-enabled security incident occurs before the midterms — the order may survive as a political settlement. If either condition fails, the pressure for mandatory legislation, from both the national security right and the safety left, will be difficult to contain.
