Iran Ceasefire on a Knife's Edge: Strait of Hormuz, Lebanon Linkage Threaten Peace Deal

Iran suspended US negotiations and threatened to fully close the Strait of Hormuz on June 1 after Israeli strikes in Lebanon; talks appear back on track but a permanent deal remains elusive, keeping energy markets and global shipping on high alert.
▾

The US-Iran war, which began on February 28 when US and Israeli forces launched large-scale strikes on Iran — including the assassination of Supreme Leader Khamenei — has entered a critical diplomatic juncture. Iran suspended ceasefire negotiations on June 1, vowing to 'completely' block the Strait of Hormuz in retaliation for Israel's ongoing military offensive in Lebanon, which Tehran insists constitutes a ceasefire violation. Oil prices jumped more than 7% on Tasnim's report before partially recovering. A regional source subsequently told CNN that talks were back on track, with Trump telling ABC News he believes a Hormuz deal is reachable 'over the next week.' Pakistan's Deputy PM Ishaq Dar was scheduled to meet Secretary of State Rubio in Washington this week to continue mediation.

The broader peace architecture remains fragile. On May 28, US and Iranian negotiators reached a preliminary memorandum of understanding to extend the ceasefire 60 days and begin nuclear negotiations, but the deal still requires Trump's final approval. Key sticking points include Iran's uranium enrichment program, sanctions relief, and the linkage between Lebanon/Hezbollah and the Iran-US track — a tension that exploded again on June 1 when Netanyahu ordered strikes on Beirut suburbs, prompting a reportedly heated call with Trump. Iran insists the ceasefire covers all fronts; Israel says it does not.

The economic toll is mounting globally. Maritime traffic through the Strait of Hormuz — which carried 20% of world oil and LNG in peacetime — remains far below pre-war levels, with a US blockade on Iranian ports compounding the disruption. Brent crude was above $100/barrel in mid-May, up roughly 44% since the war's start. Chinese exporters have been squeezed both by US tariffs and the energy shock, with shipping routes disrupted and Gulf demand weakening.

For markets, the core risk is a breakdown in talks that leads to full Hormuz closure, which would push oil toward $130–$150 and detonate already-elevated global inflation. The Fed's hands are tied: new Chair Kevin Warsh inherits an economy where April CPI printed 3.8% — the highest in nearly three years — and markets are pricing virtually no rate cuts in 2026. Watch for whether Trump signs an Iran MOU this week and whether the Lebanon front quiets enough to hold the negotiating track together.

Trump Signs AI Frontier Model EO; Altman Hits Capitol Hill to Shape the Follow-Through

Trump's June 2 executive order creates a voluntary framework for government to preview frontier AI models 30 days before release; Altman spent Wednesday lobbying both parties to keep it voluntary and increase Commerce Department testing funding.
▾

President Trump signed the 'Promoting Advanced Artificial Intelligence Innovation and Security' executive order on Tuesday, June 2 — quietly, in private, without the tech CEO ceremony he had previously planned. The order directs NSA, CISA, Treasury, and Commerce to build a classified benchmarking process within 60 days to identify 'covered frontier models.' AI developers are then asked, on a voluntary basis, to give the government access to those models up to 30 days before public release, and to let the government help select trusted early partners. Critically, the order explicitly states it does not authorize any mandatory licensing, preclearance, or permitting requirements — a direct concession to industry pressure. Reports indicate that Musk, Zuckerberg, and Sacks lobbied against an earlier, more prescriptive version of the order.

The background that animated the EO is Anthropic's Claude Mythos Preview model, which in April demonstrated what NBC News called 'superhuman ability' to find critical vulnerabilities in widely-used operating systems — sending what the White House described as shockwaves through the national security community. Anthropic's response — the Project Glasswing cybersecurity initiative — was expanded on the same day Trump signed the order.

OpenAI CEO Sam Altman traveled to Washington on Wednesday, meeting with House Speaker Mike Johnson, Minority Leader Hakeem Jeffries, and Sen. Bernie Sanders, as well as White House officials. Altman endorsed the EO publicly, writing that it 'gets the balance right,' but Reuters reported he also pushed lawmakers against any mandatory approval requirements and asked Congress to increase Commerce Department AI testing funding. Bloomberg noted Altman also presented a vision for passing AI's 'potentially vast financial windfall to consumers' — language that signals OpenAI is beginning to shape its public market narrative ahead of its own imminent IPO filing.

The Anthropic-DOD rift complicates the picture. Defense Secretary Hegseth designated Anthropic a national security supply chain risk in March after the company refused Pentagon demands for unfettered Claude access for autonomous weapons and mass surveillance. A federal appeals court denied Anthropic's stay request in April; litigation continues. The new EO's voluntary framework does nothing to resolve this standoff, and the Colorado AI Act — which was supposed to take effect June 30 — remains in limbo as a working group deliberates revision.

Microsoft Claims a 1,000-Fold Quantum Breakthrough. Physicists Say Show Your Work.

Microsoft's Majorana 2 chip, unveiled this week, promises a scalable quantum computer by 2029 — but contested science, China's rival programs, and a 12-qubit prototype raise urgent questions about whether America's quantum bet is built on solid ground.
Microsoft unveiled Majorana 2 on June 2 at its Build 2026 conference, making a specific 2029 commercial quantum target for the first time and triggering immediate scientific controversy and geopolitical commentary about the US-China quantum race.

On June 2, at Microsoft's Build conference in San Francisco, CEO Satya Nadella closed the keynote by announcing Majorana 2 — the company's second-generation topological quantum processor and the successor to Majorana 1, which debuted in February 2025. The announcement carried a striking new confidence: Microsoft said it was cutting its timeline to a scalable, commercially useful quantum computer from a vague "years away" estimate to a specific target of 2029, halving its previous roadmap. The headline performance claim was arresting — qubits on Majorana 2 reportedly hold their quantum state for an average of 20 seconds, and sometimes up to a full minute, compared to the milliseconds achieved by Majorana 1. The company calls this a 1,000-fold improvement in reliability.

The technical change driving this leap is a materials substitution that sounds almost counterintuitive. Microsoft replaced the aluminum superconductors in Majorana 1 with lead — a heavier metal rarely used in chip fabrication because it dissolves in water, making manufacturing extraordinarily difficult. The company credits its agentic AI research platform, Microsoft Discovery, with helping engineers finally crack this materials problem after years of failed attempts. The AI agents automated complex measurements, optimized the fabrication process, and analyzed decades of prior research data to identify patterns that human researchers had missed. In a striking demonstration of AI's expanding role in physical science, the chip's very design became a product of AI acceleration.

Microsoft's approach to quantum computing is architecturally distinctive and deeply controversial. Where IBM, Google, and most competitors use superconducting qubits or trapped ions, Microsoft has spent nearly two decades betting on topological qubits based on so-called Majorana zero modes — exotic quantum states predicted by theory but whose reliable creation and control remains disputed. The theoretical appeal is significant: topological qubits are designed to be inherently more resistant to errors, which is the central engineering bottleneck in quantum computing. In principle, this architecture could scale better than others, potentially squeezing millions of qubits onto a single chip. The present Majorana 2 chip, by contrast, has just 12 qubits — a humbling number given that a commercially useful quantum machine would need millions.

The scientific community's reaction has been pointed. Physicists at the University of St. Andrews and elsewhere have stated publicly that Microsoft has not released sufficient data for independent verification of its claims regarding Majorana-based quantum states. The results were published as a preprint — not yet peer-reviewed — and critics argue that the same data-transparency gaps that plagued Majorana 1's announcement persist in the Majorana 2 paper. Microsoft's executives counter that trade secrets prevent full disclosure, but say they have shared findings in confidential sessions with the U.S. Defense Advanced Research Projects Agency (DARPA), which has advanced Microsoft to the final phase of its utility-scale quantum computing evaluation program. That DARPA imprimatur gives the announcement some institutional credibility, even as peer review remains pending.

The geopolitical stakes around this announcement are hard to overstate. China leads global public investment in quantum technologies, having poured more than $15 billion into the field, compared to the United States' significantly smaller federal commitment. Beijing's quantum strategy is explicitly state-driven: close integration between government research labs, defense-affiliated firms, and the People's Liberation Army creates direct pathways from scientific breakthroughs to military procurement. The US-China Economic and Security Review Commission has warned that China is "aggressively pursuing cryptographically relevant quantum computing" and deliberately obscuring the true state of its most advanced programs. The country that achieves practical quantum computing first could gain the ability to break widely used encryption methods — compromising financial systems, military communications, and intelligence infrastructure globally. Microsoft's Brad Smith has explicitly warned of a "race the US can't afford to lose" in the wake of China's own quantum milestone announcements.

The interpretive divide is genuine and consequential. Optimists read Majorana 2 as validation of a high-risk, high-reward architecture that, if it works, could leapfrog competitors who are grinding away at incremental qubit count improvements with more orthodox approaches. Skeptics see a pattern of bold claims inadequately supported by reproducible public data — and worry that the US quantum strategy is over-indexed on a single company's contested approach at the expense of broader, better-verified programs. The gap between a 12-qubit lab demonstration and a commercially useful machine requiring millions of qubits is vast, and the 2029 target implicitly assumes that every major engineering challenge along the way will yield on schedule.
