Watch for whether Trump's diplomatic holding pattern breaks, and whether the House war powers rebuke forces a White House policy recalibration.

Trump Signs AI Executive Order: 30-Day Government Preview of Frontier Models

Trump signed a landmark executive order on June 2 creating a voluntary framework for AI developers to give the US government advance access to the most powerful frontier models — triggered directly by Anthropic's Mythos demonstrating superhuman vulnerability-finding capabilities.
▾

President Trump signed 'Promoting Advanced Artificial Intelligence Innovation and Security' on June 2, quietly and in private, after postponing a signing ceremony with tech CEOs weeks earlier because he 'didn't like certain aspects of it.' The order asks AI developers, on a voluntary basis, to submit their most capable 'covered frontier models' to a classified government benchmarking process run by the NSA and CISA, and to provide access up to 30 days before broader release. The order expressly states it creates no mandatory licensing or permitting requirement — a line clearly written to preempt industry pushback from Zuckerberg, Musk, and Sacks, all of whom reportedly lobbied against an earlier, stricter draft.

The trigger for the order is not hypothetical. In April, Anthropic's Mythos Preview model demonstrated a superhuman ability to find critical zero-day vulnerabilities in widely-used operating systems — capabilities NBC News described as sending 'shockwaves through Washington.' The order builds an entire cybersecurity architecture around the premise that frontier AI is simultaneously a national security asset and a national security threat. The NSA will lead classification of which models qualify, and CISA must issue Binding Operational Directives to speed up AI-enabled cyber defense of civilian federal systems.

Industry reaction has been cautiously positive — the order avoids the regulatory teeth that critics feared — but the underlying dynamic is significant: Washington has now formalized its expectation of first-look access to AI systems that could break critical infrastructure. The Anthropic–DOD supply-chain-risk designation remains live, with Anthropic's lawsuit against the Trump administration still in litigation. The Colorado AI Act is also approaching its June 30 effective date, and a March working group draft may reset it to January 2027 — adding further complexity to the federal-versus-state AI governance battle.

Nvidia Enters PC Market with RTX Spark; Challenges Intel, AMD, Qualcomm on Home Turf

Nvidia's RTX Spark superchip — unveiled at Computex Taipei on June 1 — marks Jensen Huang's bid to own the full AI stack from data center to laptop, combining a Blackwell GPU and MediaTek CPU with 128GB unified memory capable of running 120B-parameter models entirely on-device.
▾

At Computex in Taipei on June 1, Nvidia CEO Jensen Huang unveiled the RTX Spark superchip, a combined CPU-GPU SoC built in partnership with Taiwan's MediaTek, running on TSMC 3-nanometer process technology. The chip delivers 1 petaflop of AI performance and up to 128GB of unified memory, enabling on-device inference of 120-billion-parameter LLMs with million-token context windows. Devices ship this fall from Microsoft Surface, Dell, HP, ASUS, Lenovo, and MSI, with Acer and GIGABYTE to follow — all running Windows for Arm. Huang also confirmed Nvidia's Vera CPU for data centers is in full production, with OpenAI, Anthropic, and SpaceX as early adopters.

Simultaneously, Google DeepMind released Gemma 4 12B — an open-source multimodal model targeting the same 16GB VRAM threshold that RTX Spark addresses — posting 77.5% on AIME 2026 and 94.9% on DocVQA with a 256,000-token context window. Microsoft unveiled Project Polaris, an in-house coding model replacing GPT-4 Turbo as the default for all GitHub Copilot subscribers from August, running on Microsoft's own Maia chips and ending Copilot's dependence on OpenAI. And MiniMax released M2.5 models promising near-frontier performance at a fraction of cost.

Nvidia's move sent shares of AMD, Intel, and Qualcomm lower. CNBC reported it as 'the latest sign of Nvidia moving beyond the data center to the edge.' Critics note that the AI PC concept has so far underperformed — Dell cited disappointing demand earlier this year — but analysts say Nvidia's CUDA ecosystem and AI credibility give this push a different weight than prior Arm-based Windows efforts by Qualcomm. The structural question is whether software developers will optimize for RTX Spark's unified memory architecture, or whether it faces the same fragmentation that plagued earlier Windows-on-Arm devices.

The AI Boom Is Breaking America's Biggest Power Grid — and Washington Is Coming for Big Tech

A Bloomberg report published today revealing federal proposals to break up PJM Interconnection — the largest grid operator in the US — has crystallised a slow-building crisis: the AI data center buildout is overwhelming the grid that serves 67 million Americans, and ordinary ratepayers are being handed the bill.
Bloomberg published a report this morning, June 4, 2026, revealing that federal officials have raised the prospect of breaking up PJM Interconnection — the US's largest grid operator — as AI data centers strain electricity supplies across 13 states and drive up consumer power bills.

Today, Bloomberg reported that federal officials have raised the prospect of breaking up PJM Interconnection LLC, the grid operator that runs electricity infrastructure from the Illinois prairie to the New Jersey coastline across 13 states. The trigger is not engineering failure or corporate mismanagement — it is the AI data center boom. New hyperscale facilities are straining power supplies across PJM's entire territory, pushing up electricity prices and generating a political backlash that now reaches from local planning boards in Northern Virginia all the way to the US Senate.

The numbers behind the story are staggering. Consumers in PJM's mid-Atlantic region have paid or will pay over $21 billion in capacity costs across just three capacity auctions — July 2024, July 2025, and December 2025 — largely attributed to projected data center demand. By 2028, the average household in PJM is projected to see their energy bills rise by $70 per month because of data centers, translating to an additional $840 per year. Grid operators warn the situation is about to worsen: starting in summer 2026, PJM will have just enough power to maintain grid reliability, and data centers are connecting faster than new generation can be built. If nothing changes, the region may fall below reliability standards as soon as June 2027.

This crisis has been building for two years, but the policy response has now reached an inflection point. The DOE this week launched a simulation platform called Agora to model the volatile, high-density power demands that AI campuses place on the grid. ERCOT, the Texas grid operator, has separately published frameworks describing AI data centers as 'highly dynamic power-electronic loads' that pose significant challenges to power system stability. In Congress, a flurry of bills has been introduced: the bipartisan GRID Act from Senators Hawley and Blumenthal, the Democratic-led Power for the People Act — which Virginia Senator Mark Warner co-sponsored just two days ago — and Senator Tom Cotton's DATA Act. All converge on the same question: should tech giants be required to generate their own power rather than draw it from shared infrastructure?

Google has signalled one market-led answer, signing a deal this week with Voltus to create a virtual power plant within PJM territory — aggregating consumer devices like EVs and smart thermostats to supply demand flexibility to data center operations. But a recent Gallup poll suggests the public mood is souring: roughly 70% of Americans now oppose having AI data centers in their area, and at least 25 data center projects were cancelled last year, with half of those expected to open in 2026 now facing delays or cancellation.

The competing interpretations here are genuinely substantive. Defenders of the tech industry argue, as the Data Center Coalition has, that data centers pay for all the power they consume just like any other customer, and that large industrial loads can actually reduce per-unit grid costs for residential customers by absorbing fixed infrastructure expenses. They also point out that the sector has driven over a third of US GDP growth since 2025 and is creating tens of thousands of construction and engineering jobs. On the other side, consumer advocates and state public utility watchdogs argue that the cost-allocation mechanism is fundamentally broken: speculative and duplicative interconnection requests are driving capacity price spikes that hit households before a single new data center ever connects. Virginia's electricity prices jumped 14.5% in the past year alone, a figure that strains the industry's argument that it bears its fair share.

There is also a geopolitical and competitiveness dimension that resists easy resolution. President Trump has personally declared that America is the 'Number One' country in AI and that he never wants Americans to pay higher electricity bills because of data centers — a position that is internally incoherent, since the two goals are structurally in tension. Meanwhile, China is accelerating its own AI infrastructure build, and US policymakers are acutely aware that slowing the data center pipeline to protect ratepayers could hand Beijing a strategic window.

What happens next will be determined on three parallel tracks. First, FERC is deciding whether PJM's proposed new cost-allocation framework — which would classify facilities above 50 MW as full grid users regardless of on-site generation — meets the mandate it issued in December. Second, Congress is negotiating a thicket of competing bills with very different regulatory philosophies: some demand off-grid self-sufficiency, others focus on cost pass-through transparency, and at least one floats the nuclear option of breaking up PJM itself. Third, and perhaps most consequentially, the summer 2026 peak demand season — now weeks away — will serve as a live stress test of whether the grid can actually hold. If it cannot, the political dynamics shift dramatically and quickly.
