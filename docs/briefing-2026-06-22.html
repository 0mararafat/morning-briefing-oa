US-Iran MOU Holds — But Switzerland Talks Already Hitting Turbulence

Trump and Iran's president signed a 60-day ceasefire MOU on June 17, but the first technical talks in Switzerland stalled almost immediately, leaving the fragile peace in question as this week opens.
▾

On June 17, 2026, US President Trump and Iranian President signed a memorandum of understanding to end the 2026 Iran war — a conflict initiated by the joint US-Israeli 'Operation Epic Fury' strikes on February 28. The MOU established a 60-day ceasefire extension, called for reopening the Strait of Hormuz to commercial shipping toll-free, lifted the US naval blockade of Iranian ports, and set the stage for negotiations on a permanent nuclear agreement. However, the MOU notably excludes Iran's ballistic missile program and its network of regional proxies, two of the most contentious issues.

The new deal almost immediately ran into turbulence. According to AP, technical talks scheduled in Switzerland on June 18 were postponed. Trump issued threats that further complicated the atmosphere, while also denying a reported $300 billion government fund to Iran as 'fake news.' Trump defended the MOU at the G7 in France by arguing that a prolonged war could have caused an 'economic catastrophe.' Iran's Supreme National Security Council warned that ceasefire violations would not be tolerated and that Iranian forces would 'soon respond' to any provocations.

Israel remains a critical wild card. Netanyahu publicly stated that 'we still have goals to complete,' including securing enriched nuclear material within Iran — which Israel's Foreign Minister called a 'precondition' to end the conflict. The IAEA still has not been able to resume inspections of Iran's nuclear program since the June 2025 Twelve-Day War. On June 19, Israel and Hezbollah agreed to halt fighting, per AP, though Lebanon's status relative to any broader deal remains disputed.

The economic consequences of the war have been severe. Iran's blockade of the Strait of Hormuz — one of the world's most vital oil chokepoints — triggered fuel shortages across Asia and rippling disruptions globally. Markets responded dramatically to signs of de-escalation: the Dow hit an all-time high and oil dropped roughly 33% after the peace deal appeared imminent. The next 60-day window is now the most critical diplomatic period in the Middle East in years, and Congress is already asking whether the war was 'worth it,' per AP reporting.

Fed Chair Warsh's Hawkish Debut Rocks Markets and Crypto

In his first FOMC meeting, new Fed Chair Kevin Warsh held rates at 3.5%-3.75% but signaled possible hikes, rattling equities and sending Bitcoin through key support levels.
▾

Kevin Warsh, Trump's pick to succeed Jerome Powell, chaired his first FOMC meeting on June 17 and delivered a decidedly hawkish signal. The Fed held rates unanimously at 3.50%-3.75%, but the updated dot plot — the Fed's forecast of future rates — shocked markets: the median projection now puts the year-end federal funds rate at 3.8%, up from 3.4% in March, with nine of 18 officials penciling in at least one rate hike in 2026. Warsh withheld his own dot-plot estimate, becoming the first Fed chair in over a decade to do so, and abandoned the extensive forward guidance that Powell favored — adding a layer of uncertainty investors found deeply unsettling.

Markets moved swiftly. Bitcoin fell through the key $63,000 support level, with market maker Wintermute warning that a slide into the $50s 'is not ruled out.' The Nasdaq and S&P 500 each fell over 1% on the day. CME FedWatch data put the odds of a rate hike as early as October at roughly 60%. The two-year Treasury yield surged 14 basis points. Five AI-oriented stocks, which had been responsible for roughly half of the S&P 500's year-to-date return, joined the broader selloff.

Just days after Warsh's meeting, the Fed proposed new stablecoin KYC rulemaking under the GENIUS Act — a joint effort with Treasury and the FDIC. All Fed governors voted in favor except Warsh himself, who abstained without explanation, raising immediate questions about how the new chair intends to exercise influence across the financial regulatory stack.

The macro backdrop is now sharply different from expectations entering June. The peace deal with Iran had eased oil prices and inflation fears, but a Fed now leaning toward hikes has replaced cut bets that crypto and growth equities had been pricing in. Rabobank and other forecasters had predicted Warsh would pivot dovish; that thesis is now firmly off the table. Watch July FOMC odds and Q2 CPI data as the next key catalysts.

By June 1, Anthropic had confidentially filed IPO paperwork with the SEC, potentially leapfrogging OpenAI in the race to public markets. Anthropics faces one notable headwind: a legal dispute with the Pentagon, which designated it a 'supply chain risk' over its refusal to allow unrestricted military access to its tools. HIGH AI Regulation & Governance

Colorado AI Act Gutted and Reset to Jan 2027 — State AI Regulation in Full Retreat

Colorado Governor Polis signed a bill in May that substantially scaled back the nation's first comprehensive AI law and pushed its effective date to January 2027, as federal preemption pressure mounts from Washington.
▾

On May 14, 2026, Colorado Governor Jared Polis signed SB 189, a significant revision to the landmark 2024 Colorado AI Act (SB 24-205). The amendment delays the effective date from June 30, 2026 to January 1, 2027, and substantially scales back its original requirements. The revised law eliminates the risk-based framework's 'duty of care' against algorithmic discrimination, scraps mandatory risk management programs and impact assessments for deployers, and drops certain reporting obligations to the Colorado AG. What remains is a narrower transparency and disclosure regime focused on automated decision-making technologies.

The gutting of the Colorado AI Act is a significant bellwether for the entire US state AI regulation landscape. The law was intended to be the nation's first comprehensive AI regulatory framework — covering employment, housing, healthcare, education, and credit decisions. It faced relentless industry opposition and a federal headwind from the Trump administration, which in December 2025 created an AI Litigation Task Force to challenge state laws deemed to conflict with federal AI policy, and explicitly targeted the Colorado act in executive orders.

The White House's March 2026 National Policy Framework for AI urged Congress to preempt state AI laws, calling them an 'inherently interstate phenomenon.' This dynamic is now reshaping the regulatory landscape: as Colorado retreats, California's Transparency in Frontier AI Act and New York's RAISE Act remain on the books but face similar pressure. The EU AI Act transparency rules, by contrast, are marching toward their August 2026 compliance deadline — creating an increasingly asymmetric regulatory environment where European enterprises face hard obligations while US companies navigate a patchwork in retreat.

For enterprises, the Colorado SB 189 signing offers short-term relief, but the January 2027 date and ongoing federal-state tension mean compliance uncertainty is far from resolved. Cooley and other law firms advising clients note that businesses should 'continue to closely monitor both state and federal legislative developments.'

EU Picks EUROPA Consortium for Sovereign Open-Source Frontier AI Model

The European Commission selected the Domyn-led EUROPA consortium to build a 400B+ parameter open-source model across all 24 EU languages, backed by a dedicated Nvidia Blackwell cluster and EuroHPC compute.
▾

On June 19, the European Commission announced it had selected the EUROPA consortium, led by Italian company Domyn, as the winner of its Frontier AI Grand Challenge. The consortium is tasked with building a sovereign, open-source AI model exceeding 400 billion parameters — making it comparable in scale to the world's most capable frontier systems. The model will be trained natively across all 24 official EU languages, enabling European public institutions, universities, and hospitals to access frontier-grade AI without routing sensitive data through non-EU cloud infrastructure.

The project is backed by a dedicated 6,000-chip Nvidia Blackwell cluster and access to up to 2.5% of EuroHPC's high-performance supercomputing capacity. It sits within the broader European Technological Sovereignty Package announced June 3, which includes a proposed Cloud and AI Development Act (CADA) with four 'Union assurance levels' for cloud sovereignty, and a Chips Act 2.0 aimed at building European semiconductor capacity.

The EUROPA selection comes as the EU AI Act's transparency provisions approach their August 2026 compliance deadline — putting European enterprise AI governance on a very different trajectory than the US, where voluntary frameworks and state law retreats dominate. In May 2026, the European Parliament also reached political agreement to simplify AI Act rules via an 'AI omnibus,' streamlining compliance for smaller companies.

The context is stark: private AI investment in the US runs approximately 24 times higher than in Europe. The EUROPA project and the broader sovereignty stack — including Mistral AI's €830 million institutional debt raise to build a major Paris-area data center — represent the EU's most concerted effort yet to close that gap on its own terms.

TODAY'S DEEP DIVE

Google's $2.7 Billion Bet on Noam Shazeer Just Walked Into OpenAI's IPO Roadshow

The co-inventor of the Transformer architecture — the mathematical backbone of every major AI model alive today — has left Google for a pre-IPO OpenAI, in the most consequential single talent move in the history of the industry.
Shazeer's departure from Google was announced June 18 and remains the dominant talking point across the AI industry this week, with its implications for the Google-OpenAI rivalry, Gemini's roadmap, and OpenAI's IPO still actively being assessed.

On June 18, Noam Shazeer announced on X that he was leaving Google DeepMind to join OpenAI as Lead for Architecture Research. The announcement lasted two sentences. The implications will take years to fully understand.

Shazeer is not a typical executive departure. He is one of eight co-authors of the 2017 paper "Attention Is All You Need," which introduced the Transformer architecture that underpins virtually every major large language model in existence — GPT, Gemini, Claude, Llama, and beyond. He also co-authored foundational work on mixture-of-experts and multi-query attention, two techniques central to how frontier models are built today. He is, in a meaningful sense, one of the people who built the room that everyone else is now competing in.

The biographical arc is almost operatic. Shazeer joined Google in 2000 as one of its earliest engineers. In 2021, frustrated by the company's reluctance to release a chatbot he had championed internally, he left to co-found Character.AI. Three years later, Google paid approximately $2.7 billion in a licensing deal structured around Character.AI's technology — a transaction widely understood in Silicon Valley as a talent acquisition disguised as a licensing agreement — to bring him back and install him as co-lead of the Gemini project. Less than two years after that, he is gone again, this time to the one company Google least wants to lose people to.

For OpenAI, the hire carries a double meaning. The company filed a confidential S-1 with the SEC on June 8 and is working with Goldman Sachs and Morgan Stanley on an IPO targeting as early as fall 2026, at a valuation of up to $1 trillion. High-profile hires in the weeks before a public debut send a signal to investors and potential recruits alike: the company can still attract the most sought-after names in the field even as it faces intensifying competition. CEO Sam Altman welcomed Shazeer publicly, describing the partnership as "only 10 years in the making." At OpenAI, Shazeer will lead research into next-generation model architectures — working, in other words, on whatever comes after the current generation of large language models.

The same week, OpenAI also hired Dean Ball — a main author of the Trump administration's AI Action Plan — to lead a new unit called Strategic Futures. The pairing is deliberate: one hire for the frontier of the technology, one for the frontier of the rules. The dual recruitment reads as a company that understands its IPO risk factors include both the quality of its models and the political weather in Washington.

Reasonable people disagree sharply about what this all means. The bullish read is that OpenAI has just added the single most influential living contributor to AI architecture at a moment when architectural innovation — not just scaling — may determine the next wave of capability gains. Shazeer's expertise in mixture-of-experts and attention mechanisms positions OpenAI to pursue genuinely novel model designs, not just incremental improvements to the existing GPT line.

The bearish read is more pointed. OpenAI's 2025 audited financials — leaked ahead of the IPO filing — showed $13 billion in revenue against a $20.9 billion operating loss. In Q1 2026 alone, the company burned $3.7 billion in cash. Critics argue that a brilliant architect cannot solve a broken business model: Shazeer cannot meaningfully reduce compute costs, convert free users to paying ones, or renegotiate OpenAI's reported $10.59 billion annual compute bill to Microsoft. On this reading, his primary value to the company is narrative — a marquee name that helps justify a trillion-dollar IPO valuation and transfer risk to public market investors before the math catches up.

For Google, the damage is real on multiple dimensions. Practically, Gemini loses a co-lead who was regarded as central to its effort to close the gap with ChatGPT — and the departure comes weeks after Google unveiled its Gemini 3.5 Flash model and Gemini Spark agent at its I/O conference, making the timing especially awkward. Symbolically, the story writes itself: Google spent $2.7 billion to rent one of AI's founding architects for less than two years, and he chose to leave anyway. The company issued a brief statement of gratitude and said nothing else.

The broader signal is about the structure of the AI talent market itself. Compensation packages for elite researchers now routinely run into tens of millions of dollars; Sam Altman recently claimed Meta had offered some OpenAI employees signing bonuses of up to $100 million. The race is no longer primarily about compute or data — both of which can be purchased — but about the small number of people who know how to build systems that genuinely push the frontier. That scarcity is now being priced like a commodity, with the biggest labs functioning less as research institutions and more as talent auctions.

Several threads are worth watching in the weeks ahead. Google DeepMind will need to make a credible gap-filling announcement for Gemini leadership; silence will be read as weakness. OpenAI's S-1, when it becomes public, will reveal whether Shazeer's compensation package represents a meaningful equity stake or a relatively modest salary — that disclosure will shape how investors read the hire. And Shazeer's first visible output at OpenAI, whether a preprint, a benchmark result, or a product announcement, will be the first real evidence of whether the architectural bet is paying off. The IPO clock is running: if OpenAI targets a fall 2026 listing, the roadshow begins in weeks, and every major hire between now and then is also a line in the prospectus.
