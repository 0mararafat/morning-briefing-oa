HIGH AI Regulation & Governance

Trump Signs AI Executive Order Mandating Classified Frontier-Model Benchmarking, Pre-Release Government Access

The June 2 EO creates a voluntary-but-structured framework requiring AI developers to give the federal government access to covered frontier models 30 days before public release — a significant new governance structure without mandatory licensing.
▾

President Trump signed an executive order on June 2 directing Treasury, the NSA, CISA, and Commerce to develop within 60 days a classified benchmarking process to assess 'advanced cyber capabilities' of AI models and designate those meeting a threshold as 'covered frontier models.' Developers are asked, on a voluntary basis, to provide the government access to such models for up to 30 days before broader release and to collaborate on selecting 'trusted partners' for early access. The order explicitly states nothing authorizes 'mandatory governmental licensing, preclearance, or permitting' — a carve-out that drew immediate industry attention.

CNBC noted the order was signed privately, weeks after Trump postponed a ceremony with tech CEOs because he 'didn't like certain aspects of it.' The delay itself signals internal friction: Sacks, Musk, and Zuckerberg reportedly lobbied against the earlier draft. The order also instructs the Attorney General to prioritize criminal enforcement against anyone using AI to illegally access computer systems.

The timing tracks Anthropic's Project Glasswing expansion. Anthropic's Claude Mythos Preview model — which autonomously discovered thousands of high-severity vulnerabilities including decades-old zero-days in every major OS and browser — is the proximate catalyst. Anthropic is providing $100 million in usage credits and $4 million in open-source security donations to a consortium that includes AWS, Apple, Broadcom, Cisco, CrowdStrike, Google, JPMorgan Chase, Microsoft, and NVIDIA. The DoD has simultaneously labeled Anthropic a supply chain risk, prohibiting defense contractors from using its technology; Anthropic's lawsuit to reverse that designation is ongoing.

The governance picture is further complicated by Colorado's AI Act, scheduled to take effect June 30, and by the White House's March 2026 national legislative framework pushing federal preemption of state AI laws. The EU AI Act's Digital Omnibus proposal, meanwhile, would delay certain high-risk AI requirements — a parallel softening across both major regulatory regimes.

TODAY'S DEEP DIVE

Europe Declares Digital Independence — and America Is the Target

The EU's sweeping Tech Sovereignty Package, unveiled this week alongside a new Trump AI executive order, marks the clearest rupture yet in the transatlantic technology relationship — with vast consequences for US cloud giants, chipmakers, and the global governance of AI.
On June 2 and 3, 2026, the US and EU each published landmark AI governance documents in the same forty-eight-hour window — one deregulatory and nationally focused, one sovereignty-driven and explicitly aimed at reducing dependence on US tech — making this the sharpest single moment of transatlantic AI policy divergence to date.

On June 3, 2026, the European Commission unveiled its long-anticipated Tech Sovereignty Package — a four-part legislative and strategic bundle designed to systematically reduce Europe's dependence on American and Asian technology. The timing, arriving just twenty-four hours after President Trump signed his own AI executive order on June 2, was either coincidental or quietly deliberate. Either way, the two documents read as rival manifests for what advanced AI governance should look like — and who gets to control the infrastructure it runs on.

The EU package has four pillars. The Cloud and AI Development Act (CADA) is its most disruptive instrument, creating a tiered EU-wide sovereignty framework for cloud services that, on current draft language, would restrict member states from using US cloud providers to process sensitive public-sector data in healthcare, finance, and judicial systems. The proposal targets what Brussels euphemistically calls 'extraterritorial laws' — a direct reference to the US Cloud Act, which allows American law enforcement to compel data from US companies regardless of where it is stored. A second cornerstone, Chips Act 2.0, pivots from the supply-side factory-building focus of the original 2023 legislation toward demand-side tools designed to stimulate purchases of European-made semiconductors. The package also includes an EU Open Source Strategy and a roadmap for AI in energy infrastructure. The European Union currently relies on non-EU countries for over 80 percent of key digital products, services, infrastructure, and intellectual property — a vulnerability that Commission President Ursula von der Leyen described bluntly: 'We cannot afford to depend on others for the technologies that keep our hospitals running, our energy grids stable and our services secure.'

The political catalyst is as important as the policy substance. European officials have grown increasingly alarmed by what one source called a 'kill switch' risk in US technology dependencies — fears that crystallized after the Trump administration sanctioned the International Criminal Court's top prosecutor, leading Microsoft to cancel his email account. That episode made abstract anxieties suddenly concrete: if US tech companies can be weaponized by Washington for political ends, then any institution hosting sensitive data on American infrastructure is potentially exposed.

The Trump executive order signed a day earlier points in precisely the opposite direction. Rather than imposing binding review requirements on frontier AI models, it establishes an entirely voluntary framework under which AI developers may — but need not — submit advanced models for government security assessment up to thirty days before public release. The order had originally been expected to give the government up to ninety days of pre-release review; that window was cut to thirty days in the final version after Trump pulled back at the last minute in May, saying an earlier draft would 'get in the way of' US competitiveness. Former White House AI czar David Sacks reportedly pushed successfully for language explicitly prohibiting mandatory government licensing of AI models. The result is a document that hardens cybersecurity infrastructure while conspicuously refusing to impose safety obligations on the industry itself — treating AI companies, in one analyst's framing, as partners rather than regulated entities.

This is where the genuine intellectual disagreement lies, and it runs deep. Proponents of the US approach argue that voluntary frameworks preserve the innovation velocity that has produced the world's most capable frontier models, and that heavy-handed pre-deployment review would simply shift development to less scrupulous actors. The EU's regulatory instinct, they argue, has historically produced compliance costs without commensurate safety gains, and tech sovereignty packages risk becoming protectionist industrial policy dressed in the language of security. Critics of Washington's posture counter that a world in which the most powerful AI systems face zero mandatory safety review before deployment is a world making an unhedged bet on the goodwill of a handful of private companies — and that the EU's concerns about infrastructure dependence are not paranoia but prudent statecraft, validated by lived experience.

There is also a significant question about whether Europe can actually execute. The original 2023 Chips Act committed more than €52 billion in public and private funding but produced limited movement on Europe's share of global semiconductor production, which remains under ten percent. The EU produces almost none of the advanced chips — below five nanometres — that train frontier AI models. CADA's ambition to triple EU data center capacity in five to seven years is striking, but the proposals still require approval from all twenty-seven member states and the European Parliament, a process measured in years, not months. Critics from within Europe have noted that a full retreat into a Europe-first tech approach could leave the continent weaker, not stronger, in a world where great powers need global ambition to deploy and export technology, not just consume it domestically.

For markets, the immediate stakes are substantial. US hyperscalers — Microsoft, Google, Amazon — face potential exclusion from lucrative European government contracts in sensitive sectors. Nvidia's dominant position in AI training chips is not directly threatened by Chips Act 2.0 in the short term, but the demand-stimulation framework signals a deliberate long-game effort to build European alternatives. American AI labs releasing frontier models will also face an increasingly bifurcated compliance landscape: the US government asking them to voluntarily cooperate on security reviews while the EU demands evidence of sovereignty-compatible infrastructure.

What to watch in the coming weeks: whether any of the major US cloud providers formally challenge the CADA sovereignty tiers as trade barriers under WTO rules or through bilateral US-EU tech diplomacy; whether Colorado's AI Act enforcement, which begins June 30, triggers the Trump Justice Department's AI Litigation Task Force to challenge state laws, creating a domestic federal-versus-state battle that mirrors the transatlantic one; and whether the EU package accelerates a parallel conversation among Global South nations about using open-source AI infrastructure — a domain where China, with its cost-efficient and open-source model stack, is quietly positioned to benefit most from any fragmentation of the Western tech order.
