NSA Using Anthropic's Banned Mythos Model for Offensive Cyber — Despite DOD Blacklist

An FT report reveals the NSA has embedded Anthropic engineers inside the agency and is actively using the Claude Mythos model for offensive cyber operations, even as the Pentagon's supply-chain-risk designation formally bans DOD use and litigation grinds through the courts.
▾

The Financial Times reported this week that the NSA is using Anthropic's cybersecurity-focused Mythos frontier model for 'offensive cyber operations,' with roughly half a dozen Anthropic engineers embedded inside the agency. This comes despite the fact that the Department of Defense labeled Anthropic a supply chain risk in early March — a designation historically reserved for foreign adversaries like Huawei and ZTE — formally barring defense contractors from using Claude in DOD work. The dispute originated when contract negotiations over a $200 million Pentagon deal collapsed: the DOD demanded 'unfettered access for all lawful purposes' while Anthropic maintained two red lines — no use for mass surveillance of Americans and no fully autonomous weapons without human oversight. Defense Secretary Hegseth and Trump subsequently blacklisted the company and the GSA terminated Anthropic's 'OneGov' contract. Anthropic filed two federal lawsuits in March. The DC Circuit denied Anthropic's request for a stay in April, but a San Francisco federal judge granted a preliminary injunction barring enforcement against non-DOD agencies. At the DC Circuit appeals argument in May, Judge Karen Henderson called the DOD's actions a 'spectacular overreach,' saying she didn't see how the department had supported its supply chain risk determination. A written opinion from the three-judge panel is pending. The NSA arrangement — if accurate — creates a profound contradiction: the government's intelligence wing is using the model it purportedly considers a national security threat. Analysts observe the DOD may have shifted work to OpenAI, which subsequently added safety clauses its deal accepted, suggesting the Anthropic dispute may have been partly politically motivated. The timing is significant: the June 2 Trump AI executive order separately asks frontier labs to voluntarily provide 30-day early access to powerful models — making the NSA arrangement appear to be an extrajudicial workaround of the same framework.

HIGH AI Regulation & Governance

Trump's Frontier AI EO: Voluntary 30-Day Government Preview of Most Capable Models

The June 2 executive order asks AI labs to voluntarily give the NSA a 30-day look at frontier models before release, triggered by Anthropic's Mythos demonstrating superhuman zero-day vulnerability discovery, but the absence of any mandate leaves labs free to decline.
▾

President Trump signed the 'Promoting Advanced Artificial Intelligence Innovation and Security' executive order on June 2, quietly in private with no tech CEOs present, after canceling a signing ceremony weeks earlier because he 'didn't like certain aspects of it.' The order's centerpiece directs Treasury, the NSA, and CISA to build a classified benchmarking process within 60 days to assess the advanced cyber capabilities of AI models and designate particularly powerful ones as 'covered frontier models.' AI developers are then asked — on a purely voluntary basis — to provide the government with access to covered models for up to 30 days before releasing them to other trusted partners. The government can also help select which outside partners receive early access. Critically, the EO explicitly prohibits constructing any mandatory pre-clearance or licensing regime. Legal analysts at Skadden describe the order as creating a 'voluntary framework' that 'could provide a foundation for more substantial federal oversight' but is not binding. The direct trigger for the order was Anthropic's Mythos Preview model, which demonstrated a superhuman ability to autonomously find critical zero-day vulnerabilities in every major operating system and browser. That capability 'sent shockwaves through Washington,' per NBC News. The voluntary nature is the central design choice: a mandatory pre-clearance regime would have required US labs to wait for federal sign-off while Chinese labs shipped freely. Industry watchers note this gives the government the optics of oversight without the friction of regulation. The Colorado AI Act — the most ambitious US state AI governance framework, requiring risk management programs and algorithmic discrimination mitigation for high-risk AI deployments — is meanwhile entering its enforcement window on June 30, as the White House simultaneously pushes for federal preemption of such state laws.

TODAY'S DEEP DIVE

Microsoft's Copilot Billing Shock Reveals the Hidden Price of the Agentic AI Era

When GitHub switched its flagship AI coding tool from flat subscriptions to token-based billing on June 1, it didn't just change an invoice — it exposed a fundamental contradiction at the heart of the AI productivity promise.
The first monthly billing cycle under GitHub Copilot's new token-based model closes this week, delivering the first real invoice data to millions of developers and enterprise customers — transforming an abstract pricing controversy into a concrete financial reckoning.

For three years, the pitch was irresistible: pay a flat monthly fee and let AI handle an ever-growing share of your coding workload. GitHub Copilot, backed by Microsoft and powered by OpenAI models, became the dominant AI coding assistant with over 4.7 million paid subscribers by mid-2026, generating more than $2 billion in annual recurring revenue. Developers were actively encouraged to delegate more — not just line completions, but multi-step agentic sessions where Copilot could plan, research, and autonomously execute complex workflows across entire codebases. The meter, it turned out, was always running. They just weren't shown the bill.

On June 1, GitHub flipped the switch. The flat-rate Premium Request Unit system was retired and replaced with GitHub AI Credits — a token-consumption model where one credit equals one cent, and every agentic interaction draws from a monthly pool based on the actual compute it consumes. Base subscription prices were left unchanged: Pro at $10 per month, Business at $19 per user. But what those prices now buy is a sharply limited credit allotment. The fallback safety net — which had quietly routed exhausted users to cheaper models so work could continue — was removed entirely. When credits run out, usage stops.

The backlash was immediate and severe. Developers flooded Reddit, X, and GitHub's own community forums with accounts of watching months of credits evaporate within hours. The official GitHub announcement thread accumulated nearly 900 downvotes. One Pro+ subscriber on the $39 plan reported burning through 8 percent of their monthly allotment in two hours, projecting their quota gone in under two days. Community projections circulated of monthly bills jumping from $29 to $750 and from $50 to $3,000 for developers running the kind of agentic coding sessions that GitHub itself had spent years promoting. Uber, it emerged, had already burned through its entire 2026 AI coding tools budget by April — not on Copilot, but on Claude Code and Cursor — and had since capped individual employee spending at $1,500 a month.

Microsoft's defence is financially coherent. Agentic workflows — multi-hour autonomous sessions that load entire codebases, spawn parallel sub-agents, and generate multi-file pull requests — consume orders of magnitude more compute than a simple inline suggestion. Internal data reportedly showed the week-over-week cost of running GitHub Copilot had nearly doubled since January 2026, making the transition urgent rather than merely strategic. A quick chat question and a multi-hour autonomous coding session cannot, GitHub argued, cost the user the same amount. The flat-rate model had become a subsidy that was no longer sustainable.

The dispute splits along three camps. The first blames Microsoft for encouraging a behaviour — aggressive, iterative, agent-driven prompting — and then penalising users for engaging in it. "Microsoft provided this billing method and kept making it easier and easier to burn through massive numbers of tokens," one developer argued. The second camp contends that only undisciplined "vibe coders" — developers who let agents iterate indiscriminately without clear direction — face catastrophic bills, and that efficient practitioners will see little change. The third, and perhaps most structurally important, perspective holds that this is simply the moment the true cost of AI-augmented development becomes visible — and that no vendor, not Microsoft, not Anthropic, not OpenAI, was ever genuinely subsidising frontier inference at scale.

The competitive stakes are significant. For the first time since 2023, the AI coding assistant market has a genuine pricing-driven competitive dynamic. Cursor, which has grown to over half a billion dollars in annualised revenue by building an IDE-native agentic experience, remains on a flat $20-per-month Pro plan for most users. Anthropic's Claude Code offers predictable flat costs within plan limits. Windsurf — rebranded as Devin Desktop on June 2, the day after Copilot's billing change — remains flat-fee while aggressively adding frontier model options. GitHub's pricing change has handed all three a compelling acquisition argument at precisely the moment enterprise procurement cycles are opening.

The deeper significance is structural. This is not primarily a story about one product's pricing decision. It is a preview of what happens across the entire AI industry as the shift from simple inference to agentic, multi-step AI work accelerates. The economics that made flat-rate AI subscriptions viable — where most users consumed little and a minority consumed more — break down when the product's most promoted features routinely exhaust monthly allocations in a single session. The parallel is to cloud computing's shift from on-premise to pay-as-you-go: an adjustment that ultimately proved rational, but that required enterprises to build entirely new cost-management disciplines and created significant political friction in the transition.

For enterprise technology leaders, the immediate consequence is operational: organisations that deployed Copilot widely without monitoring token consumption now face unbudgeted cost exposure. Uber's April blowout was a warning shot most companies missed. The first June billing cycle, which closes this week and next for most monthly subscribers, will produce the first real invoice data — and for many engineering organisations, it will be the first time agentic AI costs appear as a legible line item rather than a flat seat fee. The August procurement conversations, when enterprise contracts come up for renewal, are likely to be considerably louder.
