Apple's AI War With Brussels: Privacy Shield or Market Power Gambit?

Apple's refusal to launch its most significant Siri overhaul in fifteen years across EU iPhones exposes a deepening rupture between Silicon Valley's AI ambitions and Europe's platform regulation — with 450 million users caught in the middle.
The European Commission publicly rejected Apple's account of the Siri AI standoff on June 9, escalating a dispute that broke live at WWDC on June 8 and remains unresolved today with no timeline for EU users to receive the feature.

On Monday, June 8, Apple used its WWDC 2026 keynote to unveil Siri AI, billing it as the most transformative rebuild of its voice assistant since the product launched in 2011. The new system features deeper on-device intelligence, richer contextual awareness, integrated writing tools, a dedicated conversation app, and a Siri mode baked into the iPhone camera. Within hours, Apple simultaneously confirmed that none of those features would ship on EU iPhones or iPads when iOS 27 launches this autumn. The reason: an unresolved standoff with the European Commission over the Digital Markets Act.

The DMA, which came into full force for designated 'gatekeeper' platforms — including Apple's iOS and App Store — in March 2024, requires Apple under Article 6 to make its platform services interoperable with competing services on fair, reasonable, and non-discriminatory terms. Applied to AI assistants, that means any rival virtual assistant must be able to access the same deep device capabilities that Siri AI uses. Apple proposed a sandboxed intermediary framework called the Trusted System Agent — a privacy-preserving layer it described as allowing third-party AI systems to access device functions safely — but the Commission rejected it along with every other Apple proposal over several months of negotiations. Apple stated there is currently no timeline for Siri AI's availability on iOS and iPadOS in the EU.

The Commission's response the following day was pointed. Spokesperson Thomas Regnier told reporters in Brussels that 'the decision not to roll out Siri AI in the EU is Apple's and Apple's only,' adding that Apple had failed to develop interoperability solutions meeting EU privacy and security standards and had simply requested a blanket exemption rather than seeking a compliant path. 'EU law is non-negotiable,' Regnier said, comparing the Commission's position to a police officer who cannot exempt a driver from a speed limit. The standoff is not Apple's first DMA collision: in April 2025, it was fined €500 million for non-compliance with the law's anti-steering provisions on the App Store — the first such DMA fine in history — and the Commission concluded in a May 2026 formal review that the law was achieving its objectives.

The deeper tension is a genuine and unresolved technical-legal question: can an AI assistant that requires deep, continuous, cross-app device access ever be made truly interoperable without creating security vulnerabilities? Apple's argument is not against interoperability in principle. Its position is that a system capable of reading messages, making purchases, accessing files, and executing actions across any installed app cannot safely be opened to third-party AI agents without a robust sandboxing architecture — and that the Commission's demands effectively require 'nearly unlimited access' to a user's device without ongoing user consent. Regulators counter that Apple's proposals were pretexts for preserving platform control rather than genuine privacy engineering, and that the company has a track record of offering compliance theatre over substantive compliance.

The China comparison has sharpened the critique considerably. Observers have noted that Apple, which cites data protection as its core objection to EU interoperability requirements, has for years stored iCloud data on servers operated by a state-owned Chinese partner with close ties to the Chinese Communist Party, without a comparable public stand. The gap between Apple's stated position in Europe and its practice in China has reignited debate about whether the tech giant's privacy arguments are principled or geopolitically selective.

The commercial stakes are significant. Siri AI will remain available on EU Macs and Apple Vision Pro — platforms not designated as DMA gatekeepers — creating an incoherent two-tier user experience across Apple's own product line for EU customers. Developers based in the EU will not be able to test or build against the new Siri AI features for iPhone or iPad at all during the development cycle. Analysts at Wedbush had projected that Apple Intelligence and Siri AI could add $75 to $100 per share to Apple's valuation over the medium term; the EU exclusion clips that upside for a market of roughly 450 million iPhone and iPad users.

Meanwhile, the standoff is being watched in Washington with something approaching satisfaction by those who argue that the DMA is a discriminatory instrument aimed at American technology companies. The Great American Artificial Intelligence Act, a 269-page bipartisan discussion draft introduced on June 4 by Representatives Obernolte and Trahan, includes a dedicated international cooperation title and signals congressional intent to treat EU platform regulation as a geopolitical concern rather than merely a competition law question. Whether that posture translates into formal trade leverage — or stays rhetorical — will shape the next phase of the dispute.

What to watch: The iOS 27 public beta is expected in mid-July, and the general release is scheduled for autumn 2026. If Apple and the Commission have not reached an agreement by the time iOS 27 ships, the feature gap will be visible and permanent from day one of the launch. The Commission has indicated it will not grant exemptions; Apple has indicated it has no current timeline. A DMA formal non-compliance investigation targeting Siri AI specifically could be opened within weeks if the Commission chooses to escalate.
