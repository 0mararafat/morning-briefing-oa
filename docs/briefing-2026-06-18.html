US-Iran Sign Peace Deal Friday in Geneva; Strait of Hormuz to Reopen

A memorandum of understanding reached June 14 is set for formal signing this Friday, June 19, in Switzerland — the most consequential energy market event since the war began February 28.
▾

On June 14, the United States and Iran announced a breakthrough memorandum of understanding intended to formally end more than three months of war and reopen the Strait of Hormuz, with the signing ceremony scheduled for June 19 in Geneva. The deal extends the current ceasefire for 60 days, lifts dueling US and Iranian blockades, and sets the stage for further negotiations on Iran's nuclear program — which remains unresolved. Trump authorized mine removal operations to begin after signing.

The conflict began February 28 when US and Israeli forces launched nearly 900 strikes targeting Iranian military infrastructure and leadership, killing Supreme Leader Ali Khamenei. Iran retaliated by closing the Strait of Hormuz, through which roughly 20% of the world's seaborne oil and LNG had been flowing. The war's economic toll has been severe: the US military spent an estimated $29 billion, Iran assessed its own damage at up to $1 trillion, and the closure drove energy prices to multiyear highs that directly fed US inflation to 4.2% CPI in May.

Critical complications remain. Iran made an end to Israel-Hezbollah fighting in Lebanon a condition of the deal, but Israeli Defense Minister Israel Katz said Monday that Israel would keep troops in southern Lebanon indefinitely. Hezbollah and Israel have continued exchanging fire despite official ceasefires. The Senate failed Monday to advance a war powers resolution to halt US action against Iran, underscoring continued congressional division. European leaders from the UK, France, Germany and Italy welcomed the deal and called for swift implementation. France's Macron said his country's forces, including the aircraft carrier Charles de Gaulle, are already positioned to support reopening.

Even if the strait opens Friday, analysts caution that global energy markets will take months to normalize. Iran has mined portions of the strait, mine-clearing will be required, and commercial shipping confidence will recover slowly. The nuclear program issue — the core driver of the original conflict — remains entirely unresolved and will define the next round of negotiations.

Fed's Warsh Signals Rate Hike Ahead in Hawkish First Meeting

New Fed Chair Kevin Warsh held rates steady yesterday but scrapped forward guidance and shifted the dot plot to signal a possible hike — a jarring debut that sent stocks and bonds down.
▾

In his first FOMC meeting as Federal Reserve chair, Kevin Warsh on Wednesday held the benchmark federal funds rate unchanged at 3.5%–3.75% — the fourth consecutive hold — but dramatically shifted the tone and projections of the central bank. The updated dot plot now shows the median Fed official expecting rates to end 2026 at 3.8%, implying a hike from current levels rather than the cut that was projected as recently as March. Nine of 18 voting members project at least one hike before year-end; six project two.

Warsh, confirmed by the Senate on May 13 and sworn in May 22, used his first press conference to announce sweeping internal changes: he declined to submit his own dot-plot projection, issued a dramatically shorter policy statement stripped of forward guidance, and announced five task forces to review the Fed's monetary policy operations, communications, data sources, labor market analysis, and causes of inflation. He hinted at fewer future press conferences.

The hawkish shift is being driven by inflation that has proven far more persistent than expected. May CPI came in at 4.2% year-over-year — the highest since April 2023 — with the Fed now projecting PCE inflation of 3.6% for full-year 2026, up sharply from the 2.7% projected in March. Warsh attributed elevated inflation significantly to energy price spikes caused by the Iran war. Markets reacted sharply: the S&P 500 fell 1.06%, the Nasdaq fell 1%, 2-year Treasury yields spiked 14 basis points to around 4.13%, and the dollar firmed.

The political subtext is notable. Trump appointed Warsh with an expectation of lower rates, and has previously hinted at legal action against the Fed chair if rates don't fall. Warsh's hawkish signal puts him on a collision course with the White House. Jerome Powell, notably, remains on the Fed's Board of Governors as a voting FOMC member. Markets are now pricing in no cuts for 2026 and a growing probability of a hike — a complete reversal from the start of the year when two cuts were expected.

HIGH AI Regulation & Governance

Trump's AI Executive Order Sets 30-Day Voluntary Federal Preview Window for Frontier Models

Signed June 2, the order creates a voluntary framework for labs to give the NSA a 30-day look at frontier models pre-release — no mandate, no licensing — sparked by an Anthropic model's demonstrated ability to find critical zero-day vulnerabilities.
▾

President Trump signed the 'Promoting Advanced Artificial Intelligence Innovation and Security' executive order on June 2, establishing a voluntary framework for AI developers to give the federal government up to 30 days of pre-release access to frontier models before they ship to commercial partners. The order explicitly prohibits any mandatory licensing or preclearance regime, stating that nothing in it shall 'authorize the creation of a mandatory governmental licensing, preclearance, or permitting requirement for the development, publication, release, or distribution of new AI models.'

The trigger for the order was an Anthropic model called Mythos Preview, which in April demonstrated a superhuman ability to find critical zero-day vulnerabilities in widely-used operating systems — a capability that reportedly sent shockwaves through Washington policymakers. The order directs the NSA, CISA, and Treasury to build a classified benchmarking process to assess the 'advanced cyber capabilities' of AI models within 60 days (by August 1), and to establish thresholds for designating models as 'covered frontier models.'

Critics and legal analysts note the fundamental tension at the order's core: the voluntary nature means labs can simply decline to participate with no penalty, and the government 'can ask but cannot compel.' The administration explicitly framed the voluntary design as a deliberate rejection of a mandatory pre-clearance regime that was considered but dropped — the argument being that mandatory review would delay US labs while Chinese labs shipped freely. Whether major labs including OpenAI, Anthropic, and Google will voluntarily hand over unreleased models remains entirely open.

The order also directs the Attorney General to prioritize enforcement of existing federal criminal statutes against anyone who uses AI to illegally access computers or commit fraud, and charges OMB with reviewing federal grant programs to direct funding toward AI vulnerability detection. The broader regulatory picture is the White House pushing a national framework to preempt state-level AI laws, while the EU is simultaneously delaying its own AI Act's high-risk provisions.

TRACKING AI Regulation & Governance

Colorado AI Act Effectively Dead in Current Form; SB 189 Rewrites It Into Narrow ADMT Disclosure Law

Colorado Governor Polis signed SB 189 on May 14, gutting the nation's most ambitious state AI law and delaying it to January 2027 — a template shift with national implications as the June 30 original deadline passes this month.
▾

Colorado's landmark 2024 AI Act — the most comprehensive state AI law in the US — has been fundamentally rewritten. Governor Jared Polis signed SB 189 on May 14, replacing the original risk-based framework with a narrower regime focused on transparency and disclosures around automated decision-making technology (ADMT), pushing the effective date to January 1, 2027. The original law's requirements for algorithmic-discrimination duty of care, mandatory risk management programs, and impact assessments have been eliminated.

The rewrite was preceded by a federal court staying enforcement of the original law on April 27, after xAI filed suit on First Amendment, Dormant Commerce Clause, and due process grounds. The Trump DOJ intervened on xAI's side — the first time the federal government has moved to invalidate a state AI law under the December 2025 executive order directing federal agencies to challenge conflicting state regulations. Colorado's own attorney general stipulated to the stay.

Analysts at Carpe Datum Law called Colorado's pivot 'the strongest signal yet that the EU template will not be the dominant US state framework.' The new Colorado model joins California in anchoring a US-state approach focused on disclosure, consumer notice, and rights-based remedies — rather than the EU's substantive risk-management obligations. The EU AI Act's high-risk provisions have themselves been delayed to 2027 via the European Commission's Digital Omnibus proposal, though that delay is not yet formally enacted by the Parliament and Council.

The national implication is significant: with the White House pushing federal preemption, the DOJ actively litigating against state AI laws, and even the bellwether state having retreated, the EU-style comprehensive AI governance approach appears to have lost the US regulatory battle — at least for this legislative cycle. Companies should maintain parallel compliance frameworks for US (disclosure-focused) and EU (risk-management) regimes.

OpenAI's Audited Books Are Out — and the Numbers Rewrite the IPO Narrative

Leaked audited financials confirm OpenAI lost $38.5 billion in 2025 on $13 billion in revenue, giving public investors their first unfiltered look at the world's most anticipated stock offering — and raising hard questions about whether a trillion-dollar valuation can survive contact with a GAAP income statement.
The OpenAI audited financial documents were published on June 16 and independently verified by the Financial Times, making this the defining market and technology story of the current week — arriving two days before today's date and still driving fresh analysis and investor reaction.

On June 16, 2026, independent tech journalist Ed Zitron published what he described as audited financial documents for OpenAI's 2025 fiscal year. The Financial Times independently verified the documents. The figures were startling even by the standards of a capital-intensive frontier AI lab: OpenAI posted $13.07 billion in revenue — more than tripling its 2024 top line of $3.7 billion — but incurred $34 billion in total costs and expenses, producing a $20.92 billion operating loss. The headline net loss widened to $38.53 billion after accounting for a $41.55 billion non-cash charge tied to the conversion of warrant and convertible interests during OpenAI's restructuring from a nonprofit to a for-profit public benefit corporation. Even stripping out that one-time item, the adjusted loss was roughly $8 billion — still a company burning at a rate that would consume a $122 billion war chest in under fifteen years.

The disclosure could not have come at a more consequential moment. OpenAI publicly confirmed it had confidentially submitted a draft S-1 registration statement to the U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission on June 8, 2026, with Goldman Sachs and Morgan Stanley leading a potential listing as early as September 2026 at a valuation analysts expect to exceed $1 trillion. Because OpenAI has never released audited financials to the public, the leaked documents represent the clearest window yet into the company's books ahead of one of the most anticipated stock market debuts in history. OpenAI declined to comment on the figures.

The deeper story inside the numbers is the company's structural dependency on Microsoft. In 2025, OpenAI paid Microsoft $17.2 billion — including $10.59 billion in R&D-related compute fees and $6.05 billion in cost-of-revenue expenses — making the software giant by far OpenAI's largest vendor. In return, Microsoft paid OpenAI just $303 million. OpenAI paid Microsoft 56 times more than Microsoft paid OpenAI. That dependency has begun to shift: on April 27, 2026, the two companies renegotiated their partnership, ending Microsoft's exclusivity, removing the infamous AGI clause that would have terminated the deal upon a declaration of artificial general intelligence, and capping future revenue-share payments at $38 billion through 2030. The next day, OpenAI's models were live on AWS Bedrock — a turnaround that suggests the deal with Amazon had been in preparation for months.

The gross margin picture compounds the concern. Despite tripling revenue, OpenAI's gross margin declined from approximately 40 percent in 2024 to 33 percent in 2025. The reason is a structural trap analysts have begun calling the 'Foundation Layer Treadmill': each new, more capable model demands more compute to train and more to serve, so the cost of revenue rises in step with — or faster than — the revenue itself. R&D spending alone, at $19.18 billion, exceeded the company's entire top line. Internal projections suggest OpenAI expects losses of approximately $14 billion in 2026 on an adjusted basis, with profitability not forecast until around 2030. HSBC analysts estimate the company may need over $207 billion in additional funding by that date.

The competing interpretations break along a familiar fault line. Bulls point to revenue growth that has few precedents in software history — roughly 10x expansion over two years — and to ChatGPT's more than 900 million weekly active users as a consumer franchise with durable pricing power. They argue that inference costs will fall as hardware improves and that the Microsoft renegotiation, which could save OpenAI up to $97 billion over five years in capped payments, dramatically improves the long-term economics. Bears counter that no company has ever justified a trillion-dollar valuation while burning at this rate, that gross margins are moving in the wrong direction, and that competition from Anthropic — which projected its first quarterly operating profit of $559 million for Q2 2026, and whose annualized revenue run rate has reportedly crossed $47 billion — is eroding OpenAI's claim to model leadership. Prediction markets have priced the probability of an actual OpenAI IPO before year-end at roughly 40 percent, treating the confidential filing as a real but distant catalyst.

A less-noticed dimension of the leak is its timing damage. The documents surface during what was intended to be a tightly controlled pre-IPO quiet period. The confidential S-1 means OpenAI has not yet made its prospectus public, and the disclosure denies management the ability to frame the numbers on its own terms ahead of a roadshow. CEO Sam Altman has already signaled that the IPO 'may be a while,' and OpenAI CFO Sarah Friar has reportedly expressed concerns about the company's readiness for a 2026 listing. The leak may accelerate that timeline pressure in unexpected ways: it hands rivals, short-sellers, and regulators a detailed financial blueprint that would normally only emerge in a public prospectus.

Watch for the formal public S-1 filing, which under SEC rules must be published at least 15 days before the investor roadshow begins. When it drops — likely in late summer or fall — it will either confirm the leaked figures or reveal material discrepancies, each carrying its own market signal. The more immediate watchpoint is whether institutional investors who have already committed to the $852 billion private-round valuation revise their posture. If the leaked figures harden skepticism, the IPO window could slip to 2027, reshaping a year that markets had already priced as the biggest for technology listings since the dot-com era.
