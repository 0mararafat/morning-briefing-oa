HIGH AI Regulation & Governance

Great American AI Act: Congress Floats First Comprehensive Federal AI Framework

A 269-page bipartisan House draft would freeze state AI development laws for three years and impose new frontier model transparency rules — the most substantive federal AI governance bid yet.
▾

Representatives Jay Obernolte (R-CA) and Lori Trahan (D-MA) released the Great American Artificial Intelligence Act of 2026 discussion draft on June 4, a 269-page bipartisan proposal that would, if enacted, become the first comprehensive federal AI governance law in US history. The bill is organized into four titles: Frontier AI Governance, Workforce, Cybersecurity, and Research, Development & International Cooperation. Its most contested provision is a three-year preemption of state laws governing how AI systems are built — states would retain authority over AI use but lose jurisdiction over development. The draft includes frontier AI transparency requirements, critical safety incident reporting, employee whistleblower protections, and independent verification organizations. It would also codify the Center for AI Standards and Innovation (CAISI) within the Commerce Department. The bill arrives amid a fractured regulatory landscape: Colorado's AI Act takes effect June 30, and California's AI Transparency Act is also live. An Annenberg poll found 65% of Americans say government has done too little on AI regulation, including 53% of Republicans. However, the bill already faces internal dissent — co-chairs of the House Commission on AI said the draft cannot serve as a basis for productive dialogue. Congress breaks for recess in August, creating a narrow legislative window. This is the draft to watch as the baseline for any federal AI law.

UK-Japan Frontier Technology Pact Signed: AI, Quantum, Space, Robotics

Signed in London on June 14, the UK-Japan Frontier Technology Partnership formally integrates Britain's AI research with Japan's semiconductor manufacturing base and sets a joint quantum-HPC integration roadmap.
▾

Prime Ministers Keir Starmer and Sanae Takaichi signed the UK-Japan Frontier Technology Partnership on June 14, part of a broader £18 billion investment package. The pact spans AI, quantum computing and communications, space security, advanced robotics, cybersecurity, biotechnology, and civil nuclear energy. On the quantum side, it builds directly on the 2025 Quantum Memorandum of Cooperation and commits to integrating quantum processors into both nations' high-performance computing and supercomputing networks, with joint testbeds and evaluation frameworks. The AI strand includes a high-level government dialogue, support for next-generation model infrastructure, and a commitment to collaborate between the UK and Japan AI Safety Institutes on evaluation science through the International Network for Advanced AI Measurement. A notable practical element: joint robotics work for nuclear decommissioning at Sellafield (UK) and Fukushima Daiichi (Japan), where autonomy and remote sensing capabilities overlap directly with space robotics. The partnership allocates £6 million to a frontier technology research program and welcomes commercial announcements by industry partners in quantum, AI, and cybersecurity. The Quantum Computing Report noted the deal integrates UK quantum software and academic pipelines with Japan's large-scale hardware manufacturing base — a complementary pairing that mirrors the broader US-allied strategy to build non-China semiconductor and compute supply chains.

TODAY'S DEEP DIVE

Europe Declares AI Sovereignty at VivaTech — Then Hands the Microphone to Nvidia

As Europe's largest tech summit opens today with Jensen Huang's keynote and a sweeping new EU sovereignty law on the table, the continent's flagship AI independence project remains structurally dependent on the American chips it claims to be escaping.
VivaTech 2026 opens today, June 17, in Paris with Nvidia CEO Jensen Huang's keynote marking the one-year anniversary of his sweeping European AI factory pledges — precisely as the EU's newly proposed Cloud and AI Development Act tries to codify what 'sovereign technology' actually means in law.

Europe's most consequential tech gathering opens today in Paris. VivaTech 2026, marking its tenth anniversary at the Porte de Versailles, is no longer a consumer-facing trade fair. It has become the primary stage on which Europe performs — and attempts to actually execute — its ambition to become a serious sovereign power in artificial intelligence. The programme is deliberately geopolitical: sessions on AI factories, defense, cybersecurity, and energy have displaced the startup-pitching pageantry of earlier editions. The guests tell the story. Jensen Huang delivers the opening GTC Paris keynote today, French President Emmanuel Macron addresses the conference Thursday, Indian Prime Minister Narendra Modi joins as a symbolic third-way partner, and the full lineup spans everyone from LVMH's Bernard Arnault to Alibaba's Joe Tsai and ASML's Christophe Fouquet.

The news hook is not the star power. It is the specific convergence of events that makes this week's opening meaningful beyond ceremony. On June 3, the European Commission unveiled the European Technological Sovereignty Package — a sweeping set of draft laws covering semiconductors, AI, cloud computing, and open-source software. At its center is the proposed Cloud and AI Development Act, known as CADA, which would convert digital sovereignty from a political aspiration into binding procurement rules and infrastructure law for the first time, introducing a single EU-wide framework to assess cloud and AI sovereignty. Separately, France and Germany are expected this week to announce their own joint definition of "sovereign technology," grounded in measurable factors including company headquarters, data location, R&D presence, and workforce. Germany, the event's Country of the Year, has deployed its largest-ever VivaTech delegation: 200 startups, 14 federal states, 12 government entities, and two federal ministers occupying 800 square meters of floor space — a deliberate, coordinated national statement.

The deeper context is a year of rapid infrastructure commitment cashing in its promises. At VivaTech 2025, Huang announced that Europe would build more than 20 AI factories, anointing France's Mistral AI as the continent's sovereign-compute champion. Today, Huang returns as a progress auditor. Since that speech, Mistral has raised $830 million in debt financing — backed by French banks including Bpifrance and BNP Paribas — to build Nvidia-powered data centers near Paris and in Sweden, targeting 200 megawatts of compute capacity by 2027. In September 2025, Mistral closed a 1.7 billion euro Series C with ASML taking an 11 percent stake. Germany's Deutsche Telekom launched an industrial AI cloud running 10,000 Nvidia Blackwell GPUs in Munich. The EU Commission itself has committed to four AI gigafactories at a cost of roughly $20 billion. The commission is framing this infrastructure buildout with unusual directness: Commission President Ursula von der Leyen has stated that Europe "cannot afford to depend on others for the technologies that keep our hospitals running, our energy grids stable and our services secure."

But the central tension at VivaTech 2026 is an irony that the event's organizers have not found a way around, and that critics are pressing with increasing precision. Europe's sovereign AI agenda runs on Nvidia chips. The AI factories being announced as monuments to European independence are populated with American silicon — designed in Santa Clara, manufactured predominantly at TSMC in Taiwan, and allocated by a US company whose CEO is simultaneously the keynote speaker at Europe's sovereignty conference. As one pointed analysis noted this week, the GPU rental capacity across Nvidia's Hopper and Blackwell generations is fully booked through August to September 2026, with contract prices rising sharply — meaning Europe is competing in a global queue to buy the hardware it says it needs to be free from. Critics go further: even Mistral, Europe's most credible frontier AI company and the sovereign-compute champion, distributes models through Azure and AWS, and the paradox of buying independence from the same suppliers you seek independence from has no near-term technical resolution. European domestic semiconductor production accounts for less than 10 percent of global output.

The EU's CADA framework has its own structural problem. Its sovereignty tiers for cloud procurement are being written during what analysts describe as a once-in-four-decades compute supercycle. The definitional choices — which companies qualify as sovereign providers, and on what criteria — will determine whether public-sector procurement anchors a genuine European AI ecosystem or simply steers institutions toward certified providers that lack the compute muscle to support serious AI workloads. The EU's earlier Gaia-X federated infrastructure project offers a cautionary case study: European cloud providers' combined market share fell from 26 percent to 10 percent between 2017 and 2020 even as billions were spent on sovereignty initiatives. US hyperscalers Amazon Web Services, Microsoft Azure, and Google Cloud still control roughly 70 percent of the European cloud market heading into 2026.

The competing interpretations are genuinely hard to adjudicate. Optimists argue that data residency, regulatory jurisdiction, and open-source model development represent meaningful layers of control even if the underlying chips are American — and that deep European dependency on US AI actually makes restrictions on those systems politically costly for Washington to impose. Skeptics counter that the EU's executive vice-president for tech sovereignty has already stated the core fear plainly: Europe needs to be sure "nobody has a kill switch," and outsourcing the infrastructure layer to a US firm — however friendly — leaves that switch intact. The French government's moves to phase out Microsoft software in civil service systems, and the German government's cybersecurity-first framing at VivaTech, signal that at the political level, the dependency is understood as a real vulnerability, not an acceptable shortcut.

What to watch in the coming days and weeks: Huang's keynote today will reveal whether the AI factory commitments of 2025 have translated into running data centers or remain largely aspirational. Macron's Thursday speech is expected to frame Europe's AI positioning against Washington's deregulatory posture and Beijing's state-led model, potentially announcing new national investments or regulatory flexibilities for French AI companies. The Franco-German sovereign technology definition, if formally announced this week, will be closely parsed for whether it inadvertently excludes infrastructure built on non-European chips — which would mean excluding most of what is currently being built. And in Brussels, CADA begins its legislative journey toward the European Parliament and Council, with a target timeline of late 2027 for final adoption. The outcome of that negotiation will determine whether VivaTech's sovereignty declarations become law, or remain the most expensive performance of independence in European tech history.
