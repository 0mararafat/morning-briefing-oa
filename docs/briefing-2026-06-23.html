Warsh's Hawkish Debut Sends Yields Surging, Rate Hike Back on Table

New Fed Chair Kevin Warsh's first press conference last week rattled markets as nine officials penciled in rate hikes for 2026, with traders now pricing a 60.7% chance of an October hike — a complete inversion of the rate-cut consensus from just months ago.
▾

Kevin Warsh was sworn in as the 17th Federal Reserve Chair on May 22, 2026, confirmed in a 54-45 Senate vote — the closest in the modern era — and held his first FOMC meeting on June 16-17. The committee voted unanimously to hold the benchmark rate steady at 3.5–3.75%, but the real shock came from the dot plot and Warsh's press conference demeanor. Nine officials signaled they supported higher rates in 2026, with six backing two quarter-point increases — a sharp reversal from March, when no policymakers penciled in a hike. The two-year Treasury yield jumped 16 basis points on the day, and stock prices fell sharply.

Warsh struck a deliberately hawkish tone, declaring "inflation is a choice" and announcing a sweeping set of internal task forces to overhaul Fed communications, the balance sheet, data practices, productivity, and the inflation framework itself. In an unusual move, he declined to submit his own economic forecast to the dot plot, effectively devaluing the rest of the committee's projections while preserving his own flexibility. He also signaled he may reduce the frequency of post-meeting press conferences, raising concerns among transparency advocates about reduced public visibility into Fed deliberations.

The inflation backdrop justifies the hawkishness. CPI hit 4.2% year-over-year in May — a three-year high — largely driven by energy prices stemming from the US-Iran war. Core PCE stood at 3.3%. Employers added 172,000 jobs in May, keeping unemployment at 4.3%, meaning the Fed has no labor market excuse to cut. Trump, who installed Warsh expecting lower rates, now faces a Fed chair who appears unwilling to bend to political pressure — at least publicly. CNBC's analysis flagged that Warsh's task forces are his mechanism for reshaping the FOMC's consensus gradually, rather than forcing immediate confrontation.

Watch: July 14 CPI print (covering June data) is the next major inflection point. If energy prices recede following any Iran war resolution, Warsh gets cover to hold. If not, the October FOMC meeting — one week before midterm elections — becomes a politically explosive moment.

EU AI Act's Full Enforcement Clock Is Ticking: August 2 Deadline 40 Days Out

With 40 days until the EU AI Act's broadest enforcement wave activates — covering chatbot transparency, high-risk AI systems, and GPAI model supervision — global AI companies face a compliance sprint with fines up to €35M or 7% of global revenue.
▾

August 2, 2026 is the EU AI Act's most consequential enforcement date to date. From that date, transparency obligations under Article 50 become enforceable for all covered AI systems: chatbots must disclose their artificial nature, emotion recognition systems require user notification, and deepfake content must carry machine-readable watermarks. Simultaneously, the European Commission gains full supervision and enforcement powers over General-Purpose AI model providers — including the ability to impose fines, request documentation, and mandate market withdrawal.

A political agreement reached on May 7 between the EU Council and Parliament (Omnibus VII) streamlined several provisions: high-risk AI systems in biometrics, critical infrastructure, education, employment, and law enforcement now face a delayed deadline of December 2, 2027 rather than August 2026. However, the chatbot transparency rules and GPAI enforcement powers land on August 2 as planned. The AI Office's final Code of Practice on AI-generated content marking and labeling — expected by June 2026 — remains in draft form, leaving companies with limited time to implement.

For US labs, the compliance burden is acute. Every major foundation model provider — Anthropic, OpenAI, Google DeepMind — must demonstrate adherence to transparency codes or face enforcement scrutiny. Non-compliance penalties can reach up to €35 million or 7% of annual worldwide revenue, whichever is higher. The EU also agreed to extend SME simplifications to companies with up to 750 employees and €150M in revenue, a significant concession to mid-market players.

Watch: Formal adoption of the Omnibus VII text by Parliament and Council is expected by July 2026, ahead of the August 2 date. The EU AI Office's final guidance publication in the next two to three weeks will be the critical compliance reference document for practitioners.

Anthropic's Fable 5 Government Shutdown Formalizes a Two-Tier AI Access Regime

The forced shutdown of Anthropic's Fable 5 model — while 200 cleared organizations retain access to an unguardrailed variant — marks the clearest precedent yet for frontier AI as a controlled substance licensed by the state.
▾

Anthropic launched its Fable 5 model on June 9. Within 72 hours, the US government ordered Anthropic to shut it down after a jailbreak was discovered that bypassed safety guardrails. Anthropic complied immediately, cutting off all public and commercial access. However, Bloomberg reported that 200 Project Glasswing organizations — including Cisco and Dragos — retained access to the raw, unguardrailed Mythos Preview specifically for cyber defense purposes. The guardrailed version is shut down. The unguardrailed version continues to operate for government-cleared entities.

This episode creates the clearest structural precedent for what analysts are calling a two-tier AI access regime: frontier models deemed too dangerous for public deployment remain available to a cleared ecosystem under government supervision. This dynamic is reinforced by the June 2 White House Executive Order (EO 14409) on "Promoting Advanced Artificial Intelligence Innovation and Security," which directed NSA, CISA, and Treasury to develop a classified benchmarking process for designating 'covered frontier models' — with deliverables required by August 1, 2026. Crucially, the EO explicitly states it does not authorize mandatory licensing or preclearance for model development, preserving the innovation-permissive framing.

The political dimension is sharp: Trump publicly told G7 leaders that Anthropic talks are 'going fine' — the same company whose model his government just shut down is simultaneously in active White House negotiations. Anthropic is being shaped into a licensed distributor of controlled-substance AI capability. Meanwhile, developers report the restored version of Fable 5 triggers safety fallbacks more frequently than the pre-shutdown version, particularly in cybersecurity, chemistry, and biological research domains — suggesting Anthropic tightened the model post-restoration in ways not publicly disclosed.

Watch: The August 1 classified benchmarking deliverable from NSA/CISA will determine what qualifies as a 'covered frontier model.' That designation framework will shape every major lab's deployment strategy for the rest of the decade.

America's Grid Gets Its AI Mandate — But the Clock Is Already Running Out

Five days after FERC ordered six major grid operators to rewire how America powers its AI buildout, the 60-day countdown is live — and China's $295 billion state-led alternative is already making the dysfunction look existential.
FERC's 60-day and 30-day deadlines — the most significant federal AI energy policy action in history — began counting down last week, making this the live governing framework for the American AI infrastructure race as of today.

On June 18, the Federal Energy Regulatory Commission took what it called one of its most consequential actions in a generation. In a unanimous vote, FERC issued tailored "show-cause" orders to all six of the regional grid operators under its jurisdiction — PJM Interconnection, MISO, Southwest Power Pool, CAISO, ISO New England, and NYISO — directing them to either justify their existing rules for connecting data centers and other large power users to the grid, or rewrite those rules within 60 days. The orders cover about 200 million Americans across more than 30 states. They are now, as of this week, the live framework governing the energy future of the American AI industry.

The trigger is simple to state and bewilderingly hard to solve. A single AI training facility can draw hundreds of megawatts — an AI-related computational task can consume up to a thousand times more electricity than a conventional web search. U.S. data center electricity demand is projected to grow from roughly 176 terawatt-hours in 2023 to between 325 and 580 terawatt-hours by 2028. Morgan Stanley forecasts a shortfall of roughly 49 gigawatts in available power access for U.S. data centers by that same year. The AI infrastructure boom has turned electricity access into the single most consequential commercial and national security variable in technology — and the existing grid interconnection queue, with its multi-year backlogs and cost structures designed for an era of flat demand, is not equipped to handle it. PJM alone raised power costs by 75.5 percent, a hike blamed largely on data center demand. In Maryland, a group of 80 state lawmakers filed support for a FERC complaint the week before the orders dropped, arguing that ratepayers in their state would pay $1.6 billion over a decade for transmission projects driven by data centers they never asked for.

FERC's orders are architecturally clever and politically careful. Rather than assert a sweeping national rule — which Energy Secretary Chris Wright had pushed for and which would have invited immediate litigation from state regulators — FERC used Section 206 of the Federal Power Act to shift the legal burden onto grid operators themselves: your tariff stays in place only if you can prove it is "just and reasonable." The commission explicitly protected consumer ratepayers, formally barring grid operators from socializing AI infrastructure costs across household electricity bills. Major hyperscalers, including Google, Microsoft, Meta, xAI, Oracle, OpenAI, and Amazon, had already signed a White House Ratepayer Protection Pledge committing to build or buy their own generation. FERC's orders now backstop that commitment with regulatory force. Each grid operator also has just 30 days to file a resource adequacy report explaining how it intends to keep the lights on for both existing customers and the gigawatt-scale loads arriving on its doorstep.

The contrast with China is clarifying and uncomfortable. While FERC is coaxing six independent, litigious regional operators toward voluntary reforms on a 60-day clock, Beijing is simply spending. China's National Development and Reform Commission is drafting a $295 billion, five-year plan to connect thousands of data centers into a unified national computing grid, operated primarily by state telecoms China Mobile and China Telecom, and mandating that at least 80 percent of core technology — including AI chips — come from domestic suppliers like Huawei. That requirement structurally excludes Nvidia and AMD from the world's largest new AI procurement. The plan is part of Beijing's broader "Six Networks" infrastructure program, targeting network completion by 2028. China already generates more than twice as much electricity as the United States, and BloombergNEF estimates it will add more than six times as much new generation capacity over the next five years. As one analyst framed the asymmetry: the U.S. has the best AI "brains" — the chips — but is fighting over who pays for the power; China has the "muscle" — the energy — but has been hobbled by export controls on the brains.

The disagreements on the American side are real and ideologically charged. Consumer advocates and environmental groups like the Sierra Club broadly welcomed FERC's ratepayer protections, but flagged a critical gap: the orders do not apply to Texas, which runs its own grid outside FERC jurisdiction and is simultaneously one of the nation's fastest-growing data center hubs. Utilities in southeastern states — North Carolina, Tennessee, Alabama, Georgia — face the same dynamic without any federal standardization at all. State regulators remain wary of federal encroachment, and the National Association of Regulatory Utility Commissioners had filed comments explicitly warning that federal large-load rules would impede states' ability to protect their own customers. Meanwhile, consumer advocates in Pennsylvania, Delaware, and New Jersey raised a different risk altogether: that utilities, spooked by projected AI demand, will overbuild natural gas on 30-year timelines, and ratepayers will be left holding stranded assets if the demand never fully materializes.

For investors and infrastructure developers, the orders introduce a new variable rather than resolve the existing ones. Show-cause orders are directives to respond, not finished rules. The six operators will file divergent positions; contested FERC dockets routinely slip past nominal deadlines. Whether 60 days produces enforceable cost-allocation and co-location rules — or another round of comment-and-delay pushing resolution into 2027 — depends on how aggressively FERC acts on the filings it receives back. The resource adequacy reports due in 30 days, however, are underappreciated: they will force grid operators to publicly quantify, for the first time, exactly how much generation they believe they need to keep the lights on as AI demand accelerates. That data, once public, will reshape every future argument about who pays.

Watch for: the 30-day resource adequacy reports, due around July 18, which will reveal how close regional grids actually are to the edge; the 60-day tariff reform filings, due around August 18, which will determine whether FERC has to impose solutions unilaterally; any legal challenge from state regulators or utilities testing the jurisdiction line that FERC deliberately stopped just short of crossing; and China's formal NDRC publication of the $295 billion buildout plan, expected later this summer, which will set the terms of the infrastructure comparison for years to come.
