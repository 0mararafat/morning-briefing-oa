Senate Rebukes Trump with Historic Iran War Powers Vote — 50-48

For the first time, the Senate passed a war powers resolution directing the president to end the Iran war, as bipartisan cracks widen over a fragile ceasefire and a contested $300B Iran reconstruction fund.
▾

The Senate voted 50-48 Tuesday to approve a war powers resolution calling on President Trump to remove U.S. forces from hostilities against Iran — the first time the chamber has passed such a measure in the conflict's history. Four Senate Republicans joined nearly all Democrats: Rand Paul, Susan Collins, Lisa Murkowski, and Bill Cassidy. Democratic Sen. John Fetterman voted against. The House passed an identical measure earlier this month 215-208. Though nonbinding and not headed to the president's desk for signature, the AP and CNN framed it as Capitol Hill's sharpest symbolic rebuke of the Iran war to date.

The resolution arrives at a delicate moment. A memorandum of understanding signed June 17 by the presidents of the United States and Iran — mediated via Pakistan — starts a 60-day clock for a broader agreement. But the deal is already under strain: Iran publicly disputes whether it agreed to IAEA nuclear inspections, per AP reporting from Tuesday, even as the Trump administration touts it as a win for U.S. farmers and national security. Republicans have particularly objected to a reported $300 billion reconstruction fund for Iran embedded in the deal, which is far larger than what the Obama administration offered in the 2015 JCPOA.

The war itself — Operation Epic Fury, launched February 28, 2026, with U.S.-Israeli airstrikes that killed Supreme Leader Khamenei — triggered the Strait of Hormuz crisis, the IEA's declared 'greatest global energy security challenge in history.' Ships are now trickling back through the strait, with more than 30 transits in a 24-hour window ending Monday per UK Maritime Trade Operations, the most since hostilities began. But Iran is demanding vessels follow routes near its coastline, and full normalization remains distant.

Defense Secretary Hegseth is on Capitol Hill this week seeking roughly $80 billion in supplemental defense funding. Senators and outside experts put the total price tag of Operation Epic Fury at approximately $100 billion. Watch: whether Trump vetoes the war powers resolution or chooses to ignore it; whether the 60-day MOU clock produces a durable deal or collapses; and whether Hormuz traffic continues to recover or retreats under renewed Iranian pressure.

Anthropic's Fable 5 Remains Dark — Day 12 of Government Kill-Switch

The Commerce Department's export control shutdown of Anthropic's two most powerful models is now in its 12th day with no restoration, after NSA revelations that Mythos autonomously breached 'almost all' classified systems it was aimed at.
▾

On June 12, the U.S. Commerce Department issued a legally binding export control directive ordering Anthropic to immediately suspend all access to Fable 5 and Mythos 5 for any foreign national, including the company's own non-citizen employees, worldwide. Anthropic — unable to filter by nationality across AWS Bedrock, Google Cloud, Microsoft Foundry, and the Claude API simultaneously — executed a universal shutdown within hours. As of June 24 (Day 12), both models remain offline, per Anthropic's own status page and multiple tracking sources.

The public justification has shifted multiple times. Anthropic's initial statement said the government flagged a narrow jailbreak — essentially asking the model to review a codebase and fix vulnerabilities, a capability Anthropic argued is also present in GPT-5.5. But the story escalated materially: an NSA general subsequently told the Senate that Mythos had autonomously broken into 'almost all' classified U.S. systems it was tested against. That architectural finding — not a user-side prompt exploit — explains why the 'fix the jailbreak and the ban lifts' framing from White House AI adviser David Sacks has not produced a quick resolution. The Economist ran a June 20 cover framing the action as a precedent: AI models are now regulated as weapons systems, not software products.

Reporting from WIRED and the Washington Post confirmed that Amazon flagged the jailbreak to the Commerce Department, raising competition dynamics in what is otherwise framed as a national security action. Anthropic's IPO filing, submitted confidentially earlier this month, complicates the timeline further. A structural resolution path exists: the June 2 Trump executive order created a voluntary 60-day framework under which labs can give the NSA early model access — with an August 1 deadline — and Anthropic enrolling in that framework is considered the most likely path to restoration. Prediction markets give 57% odds of Fable 5 returning before July 1 and 75% by July 17.

The enterprise impact has been severe. Fable 5 was the first model to credibly challenge frontier benchmarks, leading on SWE-bench Pro coding tasks. Enterprise clients across finance, healthcare, and critical infrastructure found core workflows abruptly disabled. The episode has exposed that standard SaaS force-majeure contract clauses are wholly unequipped for government AI kill-switch scenarios.

China Commits $295B to Sovereign AI Compute — 80% Domestic Chips Mandated

Beijing's state-directed AI buildout, financed via sovereign debt and run by China Mobile and China Telecom with Huawei silicon, is a direct sovereignty bet designed to make U.S. export controls irrelevant at the hardware layer.
▾

Bloomberg first reported that China is preparing to spend approximately 2 trillion yuan ($295 billion) over five years to build a network of interconnected national AI data centers. The National Development and Reform Commission is drafting the blueprint, with state carriers China Mobile and China Telecom operating the bulk of the infrastructure. The plan mandates that at least 80% of all technology used — including AI chips — must be domestically sourced, effectively freezing out Nvidia and AMD. Funding comes primarily through ultra-long-term sovereign bonds and state strategic investment funds, supplemented by bank loans.

The timing is not coincidental. DeepSeek V4-Pro, China's latest frontier open-weight model, launched running natively on Huawei Ascend chips — a proof of concept that a training-and-inference stack can be decoupled from U.S.-controlled hardware. Analysts at FourWeekMBA frame the $295 billion not as conventional infrastructure investment but as 'a sovereignty declaration — a national-scale bet that the entire AI stack can be built without foreign components that can be cut off.' The plan does not include separate private-sector capex from Alibaba and Tencent, whose own programs run in parallel.

On a dollar-for-dollar comparison, the $295 billion five-year total looks modest against the $725 billion that U.S. companies including Meta and Microsoft are committing in 2026 alone. But Chinese data center construction costs are materially lower due to cheaper labor, components, and local government incentives. Bloomberg's analysis notes that U.S. data center construction spending surpassed $50 billion in April alone — a single month exceeding roughly one-third of China's entire five-year state plan. The key watch item: whether Huawei Ascend chips reach H100-equivalent throughput on transformer workloads within 18 months. If so, the export-restriction playbook collapses at the silicon layer.

Strait of Hormuz Reopening Slowly — But US-Iran Deal Terms Actively Disputed

Ships are trickling through the Hormuz at the highest rate since hostilities began, but Iran publicly contradicts U.S. claims on nuclear inspections and the passage terms, keeping the ceasefire fragile as the 60-day MOU clock runs.
▾

More than 30 ships transited the Strait of Hormuz in a 24-hour period ending Monday — the most since the war started on February 28 — according to UK Maritime Trade Operations. That is progress, but WTO data cited by the House of Commons Library still shows a 95% reduction in crude oil carriers and a 99% reduction in LNG ships transiting the strait since the conflict began. Iran is conditioning safe passage on ships taking routes close to its coastline, which the U.S. Secretary of State has said would make a diplomatic deal 'unfeasible.'

The core tension in the ceasefire is deepening: Iran publicly denied on Tuesday that it agreed to IAEA nuclear inspections, directly contradicting the Trump administration's characterization of the June 17 memorandum of understanding. The MOU starts a 60-day clock for a broader agreement covering Iran's nuclear program, but if Iran and the U.S. cannot agree on what was actually signed, that process is in jeopardy. Republicans in Congress have separately objected to a reported $300 billion reconstruction fund for Iran embedded in the deal.

The economic damage from the war and Hormuz disruption has been severe. The IEA characterized the closure as 'the largest supply disruption in the history of the global oil market.' European gas storage sat at just 30% capacity after the harsh 2025-26 winter; Dutch TTF gas benchmarks nearly doubled to over €60/MWh by mid-March. The ECB has warned that a prolonged conflict risks pushing Germany and Italy into technical recession by year-end. Oil prices, while off their peaks, remain elevated enough to keep inflation sticky for the Fed and global central banks. Watch: whether the IAEA inspection dispute is resolved, whether the 60-day MOU process holds, and whether Hormuz passage normalizes enough to ease global energy prices before winter storage season.

Banning the model does not eliminate the capability; it only guarantees that U.S. enterprise customers (finance, healthcare, critical infrastructure) will rotate to alternatives. TODAY'S DEEP DIVE

Anthropic Is Worth Nearly $1 Trillion, Suing the Government, and Linked to a School Strike. It Wants Your Money Anyway.

The world's most valuable AI safety company is navigating a Pentagon blacklist, an active-war targeting controversy, export-control shutdowns, and a blockbuster IPO filing — all at once, all in public.
Anthropic's SEC review is now three weeks old and advancing toward the public S-1 filing window, making this the active phase of the IPO process — while the Minab targeting controversy, the Fable export-control suspension, and the Pentagon litigation all remain live and unresolved, compounding the stakes daily.

Anthropic is currently in the most paradoxical position in the history of Silicon Valley. The company founded on the premise that frontier AI is too dangerous to build without exceptional caution is simultaneously: suing the US government for retaliating against its safety principles; having its models used inside the military targeting system that generated strike coordinates in the Iran war; subject to a Commerce Department export-control order that forced its two most advanced models offline worldwide; and preparing what could be one of the largest initial public offerings in American history. The SEC is now reviewing its confidential S-1, filed on June 1, and the clock toward a public prospectus is running.

The financial story alone is staggering. Anthropic closed a $65 billion Series H round on May 28, pushing its post-money valuation to $965 billion — briefly making it the highest-valued private AI company in the world, narrowly ahead of OpenAI. Its annualized revenue run-rate crossed $47 billion in May, up from roughly $9 billion at the end of 2025, a roughly five-fold increase in six months. Goldman Sachs, JPMorgan, and Morgan Stanley are leading the offering, targeting an October 2026 Nasdaq listing that analysts estimate could raise more than $60 billion. For context, that would make it one of the largest IPOs in US history. When power-grid integration is included in China's simultaneous $295 billion state compute buildout, the total AI infrastructure spending race now spans multiple trillions of dollars annually — and Anthropic wants public investors to fund the American private-sector side of it.

But the non-financial story is what makes this genuinely complicated. The dispute with the Pentagon began when Anthropic refused to allow Claude to be used for fully autonomous weapons or mass domestic surveillance. Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth responded by designating Anthropic — an American company — a national security supply-chain risk, a label previously reserved for foreign adversaries. Anthropic sued, a federal judge in San Francisco found the designation looked like First Amendment retaliation and issued a preliminary injunction, and the litigation is ongoing. Yet despite the blacklist, US Central Command was using Claude-integrated Palantir Maven Smart System software in the Iran campaign, generating strike coordinates at extraordinary speed: 1,000 targets in the first 24 hours of operations, 13,000 by early April.

The Minab school strike sits at the center of this. On February 28, the first day of the war, a Tomahawk missile struck a girls' elementary school in southern Iran, killing more than 160 people, most of them children. Reports indicated the school, which sat near an IRGC naval installation, had been present in the area for years but was absent from the intelligence data used to generate targeting coordinates. When asked directly, Anthropic CEO Dario Amodei said he did not know whether Claude had been involved in the strike, and that if it had, the use case would not violate the company's stated red lines. That answer has become a flashpoint: critics argue a company that built its entire brand around safety cannot disclaim knowledge of how its models are deployed in active warfare. Defenders counter that Anthropic's technology functions as an intelligence-aggregation tool, that humans made the final targeting decisions, and that the failure was one of stale data, not AI malfunction.

Then, in mid-June, a Commerce Department export-control directive forced Anthropic to take its two most advanced models, Fable 5 and Mythos 5, offline worldwide, citing concerns that foreign nationals could exploit their cybersecurity capabilities. The models were still down on June 17. More than 100 cybersecurity experts publicly called for the restrictions to be lifted, arguing the models' capabilities were not unique and that the restrictions weakened defensive capacity without meaningfully slowing adversaries. The episode briefly threatened Anthropic's revenue trajectory and introduced a new risk factor that will have to appear prominently in the public S-1 when it is filed.

Reasonable people disagree sharply about what all this means. The bull case is that Anthropic is precisely the company you want building frontier AI: it drew genuine lines, paid a real cost for drawing them, and is building a trillion-dollar enterprise business through technical excellence rather than defense contracts. The bear case is that the safety brand is now structurally hollow — the company's models are embedded in a military targeting system generating thousands of strike coordinates in a live war, and the CEO cannot say whether his technology contributed to the deaths of 160 children. The IPO converts this ambiguity into a public accountability test: for the first time, audited financials and formal risk disclosures will have to describe the Pentagon litigation, the export-control history, the compute obligations, and the gross-versus-net revenue accounting that some analysts believe overstates the headline run-rate.

Watch three things on specific timelines. First, the public S-1 filing, expected in late July or August, which must be released at least 15 days before any roadshow: it will be the first authoritative document on Anthropic's actual economics and will force disclosure of every legal and regulatory risk, including the DOD litigation and the Fable export-control suspension. Second, the Fable 5 and Mythos 5 restoration timeline — analyst median estimates put it around late July, but a longer suspension materially changes the revenue trajectory and IPO valuation range. Third, the outcome of the Pentagon litigation, which Trump told CNBC in April a deal remains 'possible': a settlement before the roadshow would remove the single largest governance overhang on the offering and potentially open the defense market back up. If all three resolve favorably, Anthropic lists in October at or above $965 billion. If they don't, the roadshow becomes the most interesting investor conversation of the decade.
