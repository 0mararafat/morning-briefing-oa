The Night AI Went Public: SpaceX Prices History's Biggest IPO as OpenAI Files and Markets Crack

As SpaceX prices a record $75 billion IPO tonight and OpenAI files confidentially days later, a once-in-a-generation cluster of AI listings is colliding with a tech selloff, governance alarms, and a Senate letter demanding the SEC intervene — raising the question of whether Wall Street is ready for what it is about to own.
SpaceX is pricing the largest IPO in recorded market history tonight, June 11, 2026, with trading opening tomorrow — a literal pricing-day event that also arrives three days after OpenAI filed its own confidential S-1, making this the single most consequential week for AI capital markets in the industry's history.

Tonight, after the close of US markets, SpaceX will formally price its initial public offering at $135 per share, targeting a raise of $75 billion at a valuation of approximately $1.75 trillion — making it, by a wide margin, the largest IPO in recorded market history, more than double the proceeds of Saudi Aramco's 2019 listing. Trading opens tomorrow on Nasdaq under the ticker SPCX. The pricing arrives at a fraught moment: the Nasdaq composite dropped 2.9% on Tuesday and AI chip stocks swung violently all week, as investors scrambled to raise cash for the offering. The SpaceX debut is not happening in isolation. On Monday, OpenAI confirmed it had submitted a confidential S-1 registration statement to the SEC, targeting a public listing as early as September 2026 at a valuation near $852 billion. A week before that, Anthropic filed its own confidential prospectus at a $965 billion valuation. Three of the most consequential AI companies in the world are racing to go public within months of each other, each filing in the shadow of the others.

The deeper context requires understanding what SpaceX actually is in 2026. It is no longer simply a rocket company. Following its all-stock acquisition of Elon Musk's xAI in February 2026, SpaceX is now a conglomerate of three distinct businesses: Starlink, the profitable satellite internet operation that brought in $11.4 billion in 2025 revenue at a 63% EBITDA margin; the launch business, capital-intensive and central to both government contracts and the Starship program; and xAI, which burned roughly $6 billion in 2025 and is on pace to burn $10 billion in 2026, housing the Grok AI model and the Colossus supercomputing cluster. The IPO's S-1 anchors the company's total addressable market claim at $28.5 trillion — of which $26.5 trillion is attributed to AI, not space. In practice, investors are being asked to pay a valuation of roughly 100 times 2025 revenue, a multiple with essentially no historical precedent.

For OpenAI, the structural questions are different but equally sharp. Founded in 2015 as a nonprofit research lab, the company converted to a Public Benefit Corporation in October 2025 — the change that made a conventional IPO legally possible. The OpenAI Foundation retains a 26% stake and the right to appoint and remove board members, meaning public shareholders would be buying into a company ultimately governed by a nonprofit whose mission may diverge from shareholder value maximization. Internal projections suggest OpenAI will lose $14 billion in 2026 alone, with profitability not expected until 2029 or 2030. HSBC analysts estimate the company may need over $207 billion in additional capital by 2030. Revenue is growing rapidly — from $2 billion annualized in 2023 to over $25 billion by early 2026 — but the gap between revenue trajectory and structural losses is extraordinary.

The competing interpretations of this IPO wave are genuinely irreconcilable. Bulls argue that these listings represent the arrival of the most transformational technology since the internet at the moment of peak commercial momentum — Starlink alone, with 10.3 million subscribers across 164 countries, would justify a standalone valuation well above $100 billion. Investment bankers have told both OpenAI and Anthropic that an early mover advantage is at stake: the first to list will set the terms for how public markets categorize and price the entire AI sector. Passive index mechanics are also in play; MSCI announced it will fast-track SpaceX for inclusion in its Global Standard Indexes, creating mechanical buying pressure from funds that track those benchmarks.

Bears, however, have assembled a formidable case. Senator Elizabeth Warren sent a 12-page letter to SEC Chair Paul Atkins on Tuesday demanding a delay to the SpaceX IPO, flagging potential accounting irregularities around the xAI merger — in which Musk negotiated, valued, signed, and closed the deal with himself — and describing the governance structure as leaving shareholders with virtually no accountability mechanisms. Morningstar pegs SpaceX's fair value at roughly $600-800 billion based on Starlink fundamentals alone, less than half the IPO target. Prominent short seller Jim Chanos publicly attacked the deal this week, and the company's own S-1 risk section runs to 38 pages, disclosing Musk's divided attention across SpaceX, Tesla, xAI, and his political activities, alongside a dual-class share structure that grants him 85.1% of voting power. For OpenAI, analysts point to the structural oddity that the nonprofit board retains the ability to fire directors and override shareholder preferences if it judges an AI model to be dangerous — an unusual governance provision that public markets have never been asked to price.

The market turbulence is itself a signal worth reading carefully. The pre-IPO tech selloff is not random noise; analysts at Fortune and elsewhere have flagged that with up to 30% of SPCX shares allocated to retail investors — triple the typical amount — and Nasdaq 100 index funds mandated to buy SPCX after inclusion, the flows are potentially self-reinforcing in both directions. A FOMO-driven retail surge amplified by passive index mechanics could produce an extraordinary opening pop or a violent reversal, depending on sentiment at the moment of first trade.

What to watch over the next 90 days is a cascade of milestones. Tomorrow's SPCX opening price will be the single most important data point for the AI valuation debate in 2026 — a strong pop validates the bull case and accelerates OpenAI's and Anthropic's timelines; a flat or negative first day introduces doubt about whether public markets can absorb these valuations. OpenAI's public S-1, expected sometime this summer ahead of its target September listing, will force the disclosure of actual operating losses, Microsoft revenue-sharing obligations, and Sam Altman's compensation arrangements — none of which are currently public. The first SpaceX quarterly earnings call as a public company is expected around September 2026, which will be the first opportunity for public scrutiny of xAI's actual financial drag. And lurking behind all of it is a policy question that has barely been asked: should the US government, which has been in quiet talks with OpenAI about taking a direct stake, be permitted to own equity in a company it also regulates and contracts with? The IPO wave answers the question of whether AI can go public. It does not yet answer the question of whether it should go public on these terms.
