US-Iran Peace Deal Text Agreed; Signing Ceremony Set for Friday in Switzerland

Pakistan's PM confirmed a finalized deal text on June 12, Trump echoed it, and a formal ceremony is slated for June 19 — but Israeli strikes in Lebanon and Iran's Supreme Leader sign-off remain the last live tripwires.
▾

On June 12, Pakistani Prime Minister Shehbaz Sharif announced that the United States and Iran had reached a 'final, agreed upon text' of a peace deal, with both sides declaring 'the immediate and permanent termination of military operations on all fronts, including in Lebanon.' President Trump confirmed the breakthrough on social media, saying the two sides had reached 'a very strong memorandum of understanding,' and an official signing ceremony is scheduled for June 19 in Switzerland. NBC News reported that the last missing piece was a formal sign-off from Iran's Supreme Leader Mojtaba Khamenei, who succeeded his father after the February 28 US-Israel strikes that killed the previous supreme leader.

The war began on February 28, 2026, when the United States and Israel launched coordinated strikes across Iran under Operation Epic Fury, destroying military and government targets. Iran responded with missile and drone strikes on Israel and US bases, and closed the Strait of Hormuz — disrupting global oil flows and triggering fuel shortages across Asia. A fragile ceasefire took hold on April 7-8, but both sides continued trading strikes throughout May and into June. As recently as June 12, Iranian media reported its military stopped a tanker at Hormuz, and two Iranian drones were shot down.

If the deal holds, it would reopen the Strait of Hormuz — a geopolitical prize the Trump administration has described as offering 'more leverage than military strikes.' Markets reacted swiftly: oil prices fell more than 3% on June 12 on peace optimism, and Wall Street rebounded. The Washington Post and CBS News flagged that a Trump administration official added 'some uncertainty remains' even after Pakistan's announcement, and Iran's foreign minister cautioned the media against speculating on deal contents before finalization. Israel is not a party to the deal, and Netanyahu said Israel is in 'full agreement' with Trump that Iran must not obtain nuclear weapons — framing the accord as a disappointment in Tel Aviv.

What to watch: Whether Iran's Supreme Leader formally ratifies the text before June 19; whether Israeli operations in Lebanon trigger a last-minute Iranian withdrawal; and what the deal says about Iran's nuclear program, which the IAEA has been unable to inspect since June 2025.

US Government Forces Anthropic to Kill Fable 5 and Mythos 5 Worldwide in Unprecedented Export Control Action

Commerce Secretary Lutnick directed Anthropic to suspend its two most powerful AI models for all foreign nationals — including Anthropic's own employees — citing a jailbreak, setting a sweeping new precedent for government control over commercial AI.
▾

At 5:21 PM ET on Friday June 12, Anthropic received a letter from the US government citing national security export-control authorities, directing the company to suspend all access to Claude Fable 5 and Mythos 5 for any foreign national — whether inside or outside the United States, including Anthropic's own foreign-national employees. To comply, Anthropic abruptly disabled the models for all customers worldwide, routing existing sessions to older models like Opus 4.8. Time magazine reported that Commerce Secretary Howard Lutnick personally sent the letter to CEO Dario Amodei.

The stated trigger was a jailbreak of Fable 5 published publicly on X on June 10 by prolific jailbreaker 'Pliny the Liberator,' who claimed to extract cyber exploit instructions, explosive synthesis pathways, and drug synthesis routes by using a 'decomposition-and-recomposition' technique on the model's guardrails. White House AI Czar David Sacks alleged that a trusted partner flagged the jailbreak to the government, that Anthropic was asked to fix it or de-deploy the model, and that Amodei declined — calling that decision inconsistent with Anthropic's self-styled safety-first positioning. Semafor also reported the White House acted partly over suspicion that a China-linked group had accessed Mythos, raising the prospect of reverse-engineering.

Anthropoc pushed back sharply. The company said it had received only verbal notice of a 'potential narrow, non-universal jailbreak' and argued the technique described — asking the model to review code and fix software flaws — is 'widely available from other models including OpenAI's GPT-5.5' and is used daily by security defenders. The government's decision to pull Fable 5 while leaving GPT-5.5 and Gemini 3.1 Pro online was seen by Anthropic's defenders as applying an inconsistent standard. Anthropic said it is 'working to restore access as soon as possible.'

The precedent set here may matter more than the immediate outage. As one analysis put it, 'the order means the US government now believes it can reach into a commercial AI product used by hundreds of millions of people and switch it off.' Enterprise customers are exposed: sessions failed and workloads were disrupted with no notice. The action lands just two weeks after Trump signed a June 2 executive order giving the government a 30-day early-access window into frontier models — suggesting a broader architecture of government oversight over AI is taking shape rapidly. Watch for whether Anthropic challenges the order legally, whether access is restored this week, and whether Congress weighs in.

Colorado Dismantles Its Landmark AI Act; Replaces EU-Style Risk Framework With Narrower Disclosure Law

Governor Polis signed SB 189 on May 14, repealing the nation's first comprehensive AI law and replacing it with a lighter disclosure-and-rights regime — a significant defeat for the EU-style regulatory model in the US.
▾

Colorado Governor Jared Polis signed SB 26-189 on May 14, 2026, effectively repealing the Colorado AI Act (SB 24-205) — the nation's first comprehensive state AI law — and replacing it with a narrower framework focused on automated decision-making technology disclosure and consumer rights. The new law takes effect January 1, 2027. The original Colorado AI Act had established a risk-based regime modeled on the EU AI Act: duties of care, risk management programs, impact assessments, and anti-discrimination obligations covering employment, housing, healthcare, education, and financial services.

The original law faced a multi-front assault: industry lobbied intensely against it, xAI (Elon Musk's AI company) filed suit in the US District Court for the District of Colorado on April 9 seeking to block it on constitutional grounds, and the Trump administration's December 2025 executive order created a DOJ AI Litigation Task Force specifically to challenge state AI laws deemed unconstitutional or preempted. The White House's March 2026 National Policy Framework for Artificial Intelligence urged Congress to preempt conflicting state AI rules, and the Commerce Department was given 90 days to identify 'onerous' state regulations.

Law firm analysis describes Colorado's about-face as 'the strongest signal yet that the EU template will not be the dominant US state framework.' Whether the resulting patchwork converges nationally or fragments like state privacy law remains the open question. With Colorado folding, California's AI governance laws — including SB 53's safety disclosure requirements and CCPA automated-decision rules — now become the de facto national high-water mark. The SEC also named digital assets and AI governance as its top regulatory priorities in a June 2 draft strategic plan, suggesting federal agency-level AI oversight is evolving in parallel with the legislative vacuum.

TODAY'S DEEP DIVE

Washington Pulls the Plug on Anthropic's Best Model — and Changes the Rules for All of AI

The first government-forced takedown of a commercially deployed frontier AI model is not really about one jailbreak: it is a demonstration that Washington now believes it holds the off switch for the entire industry.
The June 12 export-control shutdown of Anthropic's Fable 5 and Mythos 5 models is still unresolved as of June 15, with models offline, litigation live, and no timeline for restoration announced — making its implications for AI governance, IPO markets, and US tech policy acutely active today.

On the evening of Friday, June 12, 2026, at 5:21 PM Eastern time, Anthropic received a letter from Commerce Secretary Howard Lutnick ordering the company to immediately suspend all access to Claude Fable 5 and Claude Mythos 5 by any foreign national — whether located inside or outside the United States, including Anthropic's own foreign-born employees. Because Anthropic has no mechanism to filter users by nationality in real time, compliance meant one thing: pulling both models for everyone on the planet. Within hours, the two most capable AI systems the company had ever shipped went dark for all customers worldwide.

The trigger was a jailbreak — or something that looked like one. Three days after Fable 5's launch on June 9, a prolific AI red-teamer known online as Pliny the Liberator announced that he had bypassed the model's safety classifier system using a coordinated multi-agent "pack hunt" attack that stacked Unicode and homoglyph substitution, long-context smuggling, and a decomposition technique in which harmful requests were broken into innocuous scientific sub-queries and reassembled. Screenshots circulated of the model allegedly generating step-by-step Linux stack buffer overflow exploit code and the Birch reduction mechanism, a known methamphetamine synthesis pathway. Pliny also uploaded Fable 5's roughly 120,000-character system prompt to a public GitHub repository. The screenshots were never independently verified, and Anthropic disputed that any of it constituted a universal jailbreak. The company noted publicly that it had subjected Fable 5 to over 1,000 hours of external red-teaming before launch, during which no general jailbreak was found.

Anthropics's public response was unusually combative for a company in receipt of a federal directive. In a statement published the same evening, the company wrote that it had received only verbal evidence of a "potential narrow, non-universal jailbreak, which essentially consists of asking the model to read a specific codebase and fix any software flaws." It further warned that if the government's standard — pulling a commercial model over a non-universal jailbreak — were applied across the industry, it "would essentially halt all new model deployments for all frontier model providers." Anthropic identified its rival OpenAI's GPT-5.5 as a model that could perform the same capabilities the government apparently found alarming, and called the action a "misunderstanding" it was working to resolve.

The episode does not exist in a vacuum. Since February 2026, Anthropic and the Trump administration have been locked in an escalating conflict rooted in a collapsed $200 million Pentagon contract. The DOD sought unrestricted access to Claude for, in its words, "all lawful purposes." Anthropic drew firm lines against use for mass domestic surveillance and fully autonomous weapons systems. The talks collapsed, Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth publicly blacklisted the company, and in early March the Pentagon formally designated Anthropic a "supply chain risk" under 10 U.S.C. § 3252 — a label typically reserved for firms with suspected ties to foreign adversaries. Anthropic filed lawsuits alleging First Amendment violations and narrow statutory scope. A DC appeals court denied a stay but expedited the case. OpenAI, for its part, signed a deal with the DOD in Anthropic's place — a move that sparked internal backlash at OpenAI but handed Washington a compliant frontier lab partner. The June 12 export control directive, arriving while that litigation is still live, is widely read as a second legal front in the same war.

The competing interpretations of what happened fall into three camps. The first holds that the government acted in genuine good faith: Mythos 5, the underlying model on which Fable 5 is built, was architected explicitly for complex cybersecurity and vulnerability discovery work, and Anthropic's own release statement acknowledged that releasing a model "this capable comes with risks." If the classifier separating Fable 5 from the unrestricted Mythos 5 underneath could be defeated by a motivated attacker, the concern is legitimate regardless of whether the specific jailbreak was universal. The second camp sees the directive as political pressure — a second squeeze on a company that refused to give the Pentagon a blank check, deployed through a different legal mechanism after the courts slowed the first one. A notable detail lends credence to this reading: reporting from MLQ News suggests Amazon CEO Andy Jassy may have alerted the White House to the security concerns, making Anthropic's largest investor a possible vector in its own model's takedown — an extraordinary conflict of interest given that Amazon has committed over $100 billion in AWS infrastructure to Anthropic. The third camp sees something stranger: a company that spent years warning the public about the dangers of its own technology being taken at its word. As one sharp-eyed commentator put it, Anthropic marketed its product as a potential weapon for long enough that it should not be surprised the government finally treated it like one.

The structural stakes reach beyond Anthropic. This appears to be the first time the United States government has used export-control authority to force the worldwide shutdown of a commercially deployed frontier AI model. The mechanism matters enormously: by restricting "foreign national" access so broadly that the only compliant response was universal deactivation, the Commerce Department has established that a cloud-delivered AI model is now treated less like a software product and more like a controlled technology — akin to advanced semiconductors or weapons components. That precedent sits in direct tension with a Trump executive order signed just ten days earlier, on June 2, which explicitly stated that nothing in it authorized "a mandatory governmental licensing, preclearance, or permitting requirement" for frontier model releases. The administration has now done, through export-control authority, exactly what that order appeared to foreclose through the regulatory process.

For Anthropic specifically, the timing is brutal. The company raised a $65 billion Series H in May 2026 at a $965 billion post-money valuation and has filed confidentially for an IPO rumored for October 2026. Its annualized revenue has scaled rapidly, driven in particular by Claude Code, its agentic coding tool, with over 1,000 enterprise customers now spending $1 million or more annually. A prolonged ban on its two most capable models raises real questions about IPO timing, enterprise customer retention, and what regulatory risk premium the market will assign to frontier AI companies as an asset class.

Watch for three things in the coming weeks. First, whether Fable 5 and Mythos 5 return, and in what form — Anthropic's rollout of a global refund program for affected customers has been read by many observers as a quiet signal that restoration is not imminent. Second, the DC court case over the supply chain risk designation, which is proceeding on an expedited basis and will determine whether the DOD's original blacklisting survives legal scrutiny — a ruling that would significantly clarify the government's long-term leverage. Third, and most consequentially for the entire industry, whether any other frontier lab receives a similar export-control directive over its own leading model. If the mechanism works once, it will be used again.
