Five Eyes: AI Cyber Doom Is 'Months Away, Not Years'

The US, UK, Canada, Australia, and New Zealand have issued their starkest joint warning yet that frontier AI models will supercharge offensive hacking to catastrophic levels within months, demanding immediate action from governments and corporations.
▾

On June 22, the cybersecurity agencies of all five Five Eyes nations published a three-page joint advisory titled 'The AI shift in cyber risk: why leaders must act now.' The statement warned that frontier models like Anthropic's Fable 5 and OpenAI's Daybreak are anticipated to 'fundamentally transform both offensive and defensive cyber capabilities' and that advanced hacking tools derived from them will be broadly publicly accessible within the year. The alliance's starkest line was blunt: 'The timeline is not years, it is months.'

The advisory flags AI as actively lowering barriers for malicious actors and compressing the time between vulnerability discovery and exploitation—leaving defenders far less room to react. The agencies specifically called out legacy systems, sluggish patch cycles, unnecessary internet connectivity, and weak identity controls as the key attack surfaces AI will excel at exploiting. On June 10, CISA had already ordered civilian federal agencies to fix critical vulnerabilities within three calendar days, a dramatically tighter clock than old patch cycles.

The trigger is not hypothetical. In April, Anthropic's Mythos Preview model demonstrated what NBC News described as 'superhuman ability' to find critical zero-day vulnerabilities in widely used operating systems, sending shockwaves through Washington. House Homeland Security Committee Chair Rep. Andrew Garbarino (R-NY) used the Five Eyes warning to escalate the alarm, stating China is 'months, if not weeks' away from achieving frontier AI cyber capabilities comparable to the US.

Al Jazeera and CBS News both covered the statement. Critics, including former CISA Director Chris Krebs, called it 'pretty alarming.' Notably, the advisory is light on prescriptive new guidance—it largely restates fundamentals like patching and network hygiene—leading some analysts to question whether the alarm outpaces the actionable remediation advice offered. The open question: whether the voluntary Trump AI executive order framework (signed June 2) giving the government a 30-day preview window on frontier models will actually yield the early access it requests.

OpenAI Unveils 'Jalapeño' Chip — 50% Cheaper Than Nvidia GPUs

OpenAI and Broadcom debuted their first custom AI inference chip Wednesday, built in just nine months using OpenAI's own models to accelerate design, targeting a 50% reduction in inference costs that could transform the company's economics ahead of its IPO.
▾

OpenAI and Broadcom officially announced the Jalapeño chip on June 24, with the first physical samples delivered to OpenAI for live testing. Early data shows cost savings of roughly 50% compared to standard AI GPUs, according to Broadcom CEO Hock Tan. The chip is an application-specific integrated circuit (ASIC) built exclusively for inference—running trained models in response to user queries—not training, meaning Nvidia remains central to OpenAI's training pipeline. Mass-production versions are slated for deployment in Microsoft data centers and other partners by late 2026, with Broadcom expecting the overall deployment scale in 2027 to surpass its prior 1.3-gigawatt forecast.

The speed of development is the headline within the headline: the chip was co-developed in approximately nine months, with OpenAI President Greg Brockman telling CNBC that the company's own AI models dramatically accelerated the design process. 'The degree to which our models have been able to accelerate it was very surprising to us,' Brockman said. This software-hardware feedback loop compressed what is typically a multi-year process.

The strategic logic is existential. OpenAI burns roughly $3.7 billion per quarter, with inference being its largest cost. A chip that halves inference costs doesn't just save money—it changes the unit economics of serving 400 million-plus ChatGPT users and the viability of free-tier access that underpins the company's emerging $100 billion advertising ambitions. Jalapeño joins a growing wave of custom AI silicon from Google's TPUs, Amazon's Trainium, and Microsoft's Maia 200, with Qualcomm simultaneously announcing a $3.9 billion all-stock acquisition of AI software startup Modular to challenge Nvidia's CUDA dominance from the software stack side.

Business Standard and CNBC both reported the announcement. Analysts at Constellation Research called it 'a shot in front of the bow for Nvidia.' The key watch: whether the custom silicon can pay off fast enough for OpenAI's pre-IPO financials, and whether Broadcom's shares—already up 10% in 2026—continue to benefit as the hyperscaler custom silicon wave accelerates.

HIGH Digital Finance

Bank of England Drops £20K Stablecoin Cap, Sets £40B Issuer Ceiling

The BoE executed a major U-turn on its stablecoin rules this week, scrapping proposed individual holding limits after fierce industry pushback and replacing them with a per-coin issuance guardrail, clearing a path for regulated sterling stablecoins in 2027—but leaving the UK still three years behind the EU's MiCA framework.
▾

On June 22, the Bank of England published its final policy statement and draft Code of Practice for systemic sterling stablecoin issuers. The headline change: the BoE dropped its controversial proposal to cap individual stablecoin holdings at £20,000 (roughly $27,200) and business holdings at £10 million, replacing both with a temporary £40 billion (~$53 billion) issuance guardrail applied at the coin level rather than the wallet level. The BoE also increased the permissible share of backing reserves held in short-term UK government bonds to 70% from 60%, with the remaining 30% required in non-interest-bearing central bank deposits.

The reversal came after sustained industry pressure. A cross-party House of Lords Financial Services Regulation Committee report published June 3 and submissions from payment firms, banks, and prospective issuers all made the same argument: wallet-level limits are operationally impossible to enforce across trading venues and structurally incompatible with a payment instrument that needs to move freely. Deputy Governor Sarah Breeden, who had previously signaled openness to alternatives, called the revised framework 'a major milestone.'

The framework will be jointly enforced by the BoE and the Financial Conduct Authority. A consultation on the draft Code of Practice is open until September 22, 2026, with finalization expected by year-end, positioning regulated sterling stablecoins for a 2027 launch. Bloomberg and American Banker covered the announcement. The political backdrop is competitive pressure: US dollar-backed stablecoins account for roughly 99% of global stablecoin supply, and the US GENIUS Act has been live since July 2025. The EU's MiCA stablecoin rules have been in force since June 2024, meaning the UK's 2027 target puts it approximately three years behind the EU.

Watch: Whether the £40B per-coin cap and reserve drag make sterling stablecoins commercially viable relative to dollar alternatives; whether the US CLARITY Act passes the full Senate before the July recess (White House crypto advisor Patrick Witt expressed optimism about a pre-July 4 passage); and how Binance's deadline to secure an EU MiCA home base by July 1 reshapes the European competitive landscape.

Colorado AI Act Replaced — Federal vs. State AI Law Clash Now Open Question

Colorado gutted and replaced its landmark AI Act in May under combined federal and industry pressure, delaying its rebooted disclosure-only framework to January 1, 2027, and signaling that the EU-style risk-based AI regulation model will not take hold in the US—at least not at the state level.
▾

Colorado Governor Polis signed Senate Bill 26-189 on May 14, 2026, effectively repealing the original Colorado AI Act—the first comprehensive state AI law in the US—and replacing it with a narrower framework focused on disclosure and transparency around automated decision-making technology (ADMT), with a new effective date of January 1, 2027. The rewrite eliminates the original law's EU-modeled duty of care to prevent algorithmic discrimination, deployer risk management programs, and impact assessment requirements that had drawn fierce opposition from both the tech industry and the Trump administration.

The speed of the collapse was striking. The original AI Act had been enacted in 2024, survived two legislative sessions and a special session, and withstood multiple delays—but a two-week legislative sprint culminating in a 57-6 House vote and 34-1 Senate vote finished it. The proximate trigger was xAI's April 9 lawsuit arguing the law unconstitutionally compelled AI developers to embed state-preferred viewpoints into their models, combined with DOJ AI Litigation Task Force intervention in support of xAI and a federal magistrate judge staying enforcement on April 27.

Law analysts at Carpe Datum Law described Colorado's reversal as 'the strongest signal yet that the EU template will not be the dominant US state framework.' With the original act's June 30, 2026 effective date now essentially moot (the replacement takes effect January 1, 2027), compliance teams face a narrower but still real set of obligations around ADMT disclosures in employment, housing, healthcare, insurance, education, and financial services.

The federal preemption question remains legally unresolved. Congress has not passed preemptive federal AI legislation, and the Trump EO framework cannot on its own override state law. The DOJ AI Litigation Task Force is the active enforcement arm. Watch: whether other states follow Colorado's retreat from the EU model; whether the TRUMP AMERICA AI Act or any federal legislation advances before midterms; and whether California's more modest ADMT rules (effective January 1, 2027) face similar federal pressure.

In six months, when Broadcom ships Jalapeño silicon to every hyperscaler, the same AI systems that discovered superhuman zero-day vulnerabilities in Anthropic's April tests will be running on commodity hardware in thousands of enterprise data centers. OpenAI and Broadcom have just handed offensive actors a seven-year acceleration curve. Anthropic Accuses Alibaba of Stealing Its AI Soul: 29 Million Exchanges, 25,000 Fake Accounts, One Letter to Washington

In the largest known distillation attack on any US AI lab, operators linked to Alibaba's Qwen lab systematically harvested Claude's most valuable capabilities over six weeks — and Anthropic is now asking Congress and the White House to treat the practice as a national security emergency.
Bloomberg, Reuters, and CNBC published Anthropic's letter to Congress and the White House just hours ago, on June 24–25, making this a story breaking overnight into today.

On the evening of June 24, Bloomberg reported that Anthropic had sent a letter to the US Senate Banking Committee and senior White House officials accusing Chinese technology giant Alibaba of orchestrating a sweeping, industrial-scale campaign to extract the core capabilities of its Claude AI model. The letter, which was also obtained by Reuters and CNBC, described what Anthropic called the largest known distillation attack in its history.

The numbers are staggering in their precision. Between April 22 and June 5, 2026, operators linked to Alibaba's Qwen AI lab generated more than 28.8 million exchanges with Claude through nearly 25,000 fraudulent accounts. The operation specifically targeted Claude's most commercially valuable and strategically sensitive skills — software engineering and agentic reasoning — the very capabilities that sit at the heart of Anthropic's frontier Mythos Preview model. Anthropic told the senators that Alibaba was using a technique known as adversarial distillation: systematically feeding carefully constructed prompts to a frontier model, harvesting its responses, and using those outputs to train a cheaper rival system that approximates the original's performance at a fraction of the development cost.

This is not the first time Anthropic has raised the alarm about distillation. In February 2026, the company disclosed a separate wave of campaigns by Chinese AI startups DeepSeek, Moonshot AI, and MiniMax, documenting roughly 24,000 fraudulent accounts and more than 16 million exchanges. That disclosure was striking. The Alibaba operation is larger than all three of those prior campaigns combined. Anthropic has now named four Chinese labs as distillers of its technology, with the Alibaba accusation by far the most serious in scale.

The geopolitical timing is freighted with irony. Anthropic sent its letter to Congress on June 10. Two days later, the Commerce Department imposed controversial export control restrictions on Anthropic's own frontier models — Fable 5 and Mythos 5 — citing national security concerns about foreign access. The company was forced to disable those models globally, and as of today, after meetings between Anthropic's top technical staff and White House officials, little progress has been made toward restoring service. Anthropic is thus simultaneously asking Washington to shield its technology from Chinese extraction and fighting the same government's decision to lock down that technology entirely. Its letter to senators is a calculated attempt to separate those two issues — arguing that protecting US models from distillation and allowing them to be deployed commercially are complementary, not contradictory, goals.

The legislative response has been swift. Senators Bill Hagerty and Andy Kim plan to introduce an amendment to must-pass defense legislation that would blacklist or sanction Chinese firms found to be improperly accessing US AI model outputs. A related bipartisan House bill is also in play. Whether either gains traction in the final defense bill is uncertain, but the momentum is real: the White House's own science chief, OSTP Director Michael Kratsios, had already flagged distillation as a national security concern in an April memo. Anthropic pointedly noted in its letter that the Alibaba campaign began after that memo was published — in direct defiance of the administration's warnings.

Alibaba's exposure compounds its existing Washington problems. The company was added to the Pentagon's list of businesses allegedly supporting China's armed forces earlier this month, a designation it is now suing to overturn. Alibaba's US-listed shares fell more than three percent on Wednesday on the news. The distillation accusation opens a second front, framing the company not merely as having alleged military ties but as an active participant in what Anthropic calls the systematic theft of American AI intellectual property.

Competing interpretations cut in several directions. Critics — including Elon Musk — have noted that US frontier labs themselves trained on web-scraped data now subject to copyright litigation, raising questions of hypocrisy. Ethics analysts point out that distillation is a widely used and legitimate research technique; the core legal violation, they argue, lies in the fraudulent account creation and terms-of-service breach, not in the distillation itself. Others question the timing of Anthropic's disclosure: the letter was written on June 10 but became public only now, just as the company is navigating its own government standoff and preparing for what analysts expect to be a near-trillion-dollar IPO. Anthropic has every incentive to frame Chinese distillation as an existential threat to American AI — one that happens to argue against the very export controls that are currently crippling its own business.

What to watch: The fate of the Hagerty-Kim defense amendment will be the first concrete policy test, likely resolved within three to four weeks as the National Defense Authorization Act moves toward a floor vote. Watch whether other US labs — particularly OpenAI and Google DeepMind — disclose comparable campaigns, which would transform this from a single-company grievance into a systemic regulatory crisis. Watch whether Commerce moves to formally blacklist Alibaba's Qwen lab, a step it has so far avoided even with DeepSeek. And watch whether Anthropic's Fable 5 and Mythos 5 models come back online — because the resolution of that standoff will signal how the Trump administration ultimately weighs commercial AI leadership against its national security instincts.
