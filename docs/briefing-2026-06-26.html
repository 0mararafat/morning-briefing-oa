URGENT Energy

Iran War Aftermath: Hormuz Still Choking Global Energy, Fed Signals Possible Rate Hike

The post-ceasefire Strait of Hormuz remains far below normal traffic levels, driving energy-induced inflation to 3.6% PCE and pushing the new Fed chair toward a potential hike — the first in years.
▾

The 2026 Iran war, which began with US-Israeli military operations on February 28, has created what the IEA's Executive Director called the greatest threat to global energy security in history. Iranian forces declared the Strait of Hormuz closed starting March 4, choking roughly 20-25% of the world's seaborne oil trade. Brent crude peaked at more than 60% above pre-conflict levels in late April before easing somewhat. Even after the US and Iran announced an interim ceasefire in April, ship traffic through the Strait has remained far below pre-war levels as of late June. Prices for diesel, jet fuel, and LPG remain acutely elevated, and damage to Qatar's Ras Laffan LNG facility — nearly 20% of global output — has cut global LNG capacity, with the IEA projecting a cumulative loss of around 120 bcm of LNG supply between 2026 and 2030.

The energy shock is feeding directly into US monetary policy. At the June 16-17 FOMC meeting — the first under new Fed Chair Kevin Warsh, sworn in May 22 — the committee held rates at 3.50%-3.75% for a fourth straight meeting, but dramatically revised its inflation forecast upward. The June dot plot projected PCE inflation at 3.6% for year-end 2026, up sharply from 2.7% in the March forecast, and erased the prior projection of a cut this year. Nine officials now see at least one rate hike in 2026, with six expecting two. May CPI came in at 4.2% year-over-year, with energy prices up 23.5%. Markets are today awaiting the May core PCE reading from the BEA, due this morning, which will provide the clearest signal yet on whether the inflation shock is bleeding into core.

Warsh's first press conference struck a lean-and-mean tone: the FOMC statement was a mere 130 words — roughly two-thirds shorter than recent statements — with no forward guidance, and Warsh said the committee has 'some work to do on the price stability front.' He has previously called AI 'structurally disinflationary' and argued the Fed should look through supply shocks, but analysts at CNBC and Charles Schwab note the committee is now visibly split, with nine members favoring hikes and nine favoring a hold or cut, a posture not seen in years.

Watch: Today's core PCE print is the key near-term trigger. A hot reading above the 3.3% core projection would sharply raise the probability of a July or September hike and could roil equity and bond markets. USD/JPY is already rising toward a 40-year high in anticipation.

URGENT AI Regulation & Governance

Trump's AI Executive Order + Great American AI Act: US Governance Sprint Accelerates

Three weeks after Trump signed EO 14409 requiring a voluntary 30-day government preview of frontier AI models, Congress dropped its most comprehensive bipartisan AI bill ever — and the clock on Colorado's AI law runs out Sunday.
▾

On June 2, Trump signed Executive Order 14409, 'Promoting Advanced Artificial Intelligence Innovation and Security,' marking the third major AI EO of his second term and a notable pivot from his prior hands-off approach. The order creates a voluntary framework under which AI developers of 'covered frontier models' may give the NSA and CISA up to 30 days of pre-release access to assess cybersecurity risks. It also mandates binding operational directives requiring civilian agencies to deploy AI-enabled defensive tools, directs the AG to prosecute AI-assisted cybercrime, and tasks NSA with building a classified benchmarking process to define what qualifies as a 'covered frontier model.' Crucially, it expressly bars any mandatory licensing or preclearance requirement — a clear pro-industry guardrail. The EO was triggered in part by Anthropic's Mythos Preview model demonstrating a superhuman ability to find critical vulnerabilities in widely-used operating systems.

Two days later, on June 4, Reps. Jay Obernolte (R-CA) and Lori Trahan (D-MA) released the 269-page Great American AI Act (GAAIA), the most comprehensive bipartisan AI legislative framework ever proposed in the US. The bill would impose binding requirements on 'large frontier developers' — companies with over $500M in annual revenue that have trained a frontier model — including mandatory model disclosures, third-party audits via Independent Verification Organizations, and whistleblower protections. It would also freeze state-level AI model development laws for three years and fund a newly codified Center for AI Standards and Innovation (CAISI) with $300M over three years. The three-year preemption provision is the most contentious element: a bipartisan coalition of 36 state AGs previously opposed such a federal ban, and the House Democratic Commission on AI said the draft 'does not meet the enormity of the moment.'

Adding urgency: Colorado's original AI Act was set to take effect June 30, but was repealed and replaced in May 2026 with a narrower automated decision-making law (SB 26-189), effective January 2027, after intense industry pushback. The state-federal tension over AI preemption — with California, Texas, New York, and others all having enacted laws — now forms the central fault line heading into fall Congressional sessions.

The Anthropic-DoD legal battle provides a live test case running in parallel: Secretary Hegseth designated Anthropic a 'supply-chain risk to national security' in February, Anthropic sued in March, and both sides met at the White House in April. The outcome will shape whether national security agencies can weaponize procurement rules to influence AI company behavior outside of traditional regulatory channels.

HIGH Applied Tech & Industry

Anthropic Revenue Surges to $30B Run-Rate — While Its DoD Legal Battle Grinds On

Anthropic's explosive commercial growth makes the Trump administration's attempted DoD ban more consequential — and tests whether national security procurement rules can be used to reshape the frontier AI market.
▾

Anthropic disclosed in April 2026 that its annualized run-rate revenue had surpassed $30 billion, up from $14 billion in February — one of the fastest revenue ramps ever recorded in enterprise software. Cognition, the autonomous coding agent behind 'Devin,' separately raised a $1B+ Series D at a $26B valuation in late May, reporting $492M in annualized revenue with 50% month-over-month growth. These figures underscore the industrial-scale commercialization now underway across frontier AI.

But Anthropic's legal confrontation with the administration looms over its trajectory. In February, President Trump directed federal agencies to cease using Anthropic technology and Secretary Hegseth designated the company a 'supply-chain risk to national security' — an action typically reserved for foreign adversaries. Anthropic filed federal lawsuits in two courts in March. The company has stated that the Department of War's action is 'harming Anthropic irreparably,' and argued the designation's legal authority only covers DoD contract work, not broader commercial activity by contractors. Industry groups ITI, TechNet, CCIA, and SIIA have filed amicus briefs in support of Anthropic.

The stakes are substantial: Anthropic's CEO has said roughly 80% of its business is enterprise, and the Congressional Research Service has noted that if Anthropic loses sufficient revenue, it could reduce the number of companies capable of fielding frontier models — a competition and innovation risk. The case has no precedent; it will set the boundary of how national security procurement authority interacts with commercial AI.

Also notable: Microsoft unveiled Project Polaris at Build 2026, an in-house coding model running on Maia chips that replaces GPT-4 Turbo for all GitHub Copilot subscribers starting in August — marking the first major fracture in Copilot's dependence on OpenAI and signaling Big Tech's growing self-sufficiency in AI silicon.

TRACKING Frontier Tech / AI

EU Sovereign AI Push: EUROPA Consortium Wins Frontier AI Grand Challenge

Europe's bet on AI sovereignty is accelerating, with a 6,000-chip Blackwell-backed consortium tasked with building a 400B+ parameter open-source model across all 24 EU languages.
▾

On June 19, the European Commission selected the EUROPA Consortium, led by Italian enterprise Domyn, as the winner of its Frontier AI Grand Challenge. The consortium is backed by a dedicated 6,000-chip NVIDIA Blackwell cluster and up to 2.5% of EuroHPC's supercomputing capacity, tasked with building a sovereign, open-source model exceeding 400 billion parameters — trained natively across all 24 official EU languages. The objective is to allow European public institutions, hospitals, and universities to deploy frontier-grade AI without routing sensitive data through non-EU cloud infrastructure.

The initiative is complementary to, but distinct from, the European Commission's June 3 Cloud and AI Development Act (CADA) proposal, which establishes four 'Union assurance levels' for public sector cloud adoption — a direct regulatory response to the EU cloud market share decline from 29% in 2017 to approximately 15% today.

Germany's SPRIND is simultaneously running a parallel initiative to fund at least three new European Frontier AI Labs, with jury decisions expected by end of June 2026. Taken together, these moves represent the most coordinated European AI sovereignty push to date. The EU's Digital Omnibus, which proposes to delay and simplify certain EU AI Act obligations for high-risk systems, had its second political trilogue in April, with negotiations ongoing — a development that may ease near-term compliance burdens for companies while the sovereign build-out gains speed.

TODAY'S DEEP DIVE

America's AI Rules Fight: Congress Bets on Federal Preemption — and Is Losing Allies Fast

The Great American AI Act, the most serious federal AI framework Congress has ever drafted, is already fracturing the coalition it needs to pass, as states, safety advocates, and even Anthropic push back on its core bargain.
Congress heads into its pre-July 4th recess this week with the Great American AI Act's feedback period ending, Washington Legal Foundation analysis published Monday crystallizing the opposition, and no clear legislative path forward — making this the defining moment of the 2026 US AI governance fight.

Congress heads into its July 4th recess this week with its most ambitious AI governance proposal still sitting as a discussion draft — and the political coalition behind it visibly eroding. Three weeks after Representatives Jay Obernolte (R-CA) and Lori Trahan (D-MA) released the 269-page Great American Artificial Intelligence Act, the feedback period has surfaced a coalition of opponents that mirrors the one that killed the last preemption effort.

The bill's core trade is audacious: in exchange for the first comprehensive federal safety framework governing frontier AI models, Congress would freeze state laws specifically regulating how AI models are built for three years. The so-called "development preemption" would suspend California transparency rules, parts of Colorado's reformed automated-decision law, and nascent safety frameworks in New York, Illinois, and Texas. States would retain authority over how AI is deployed — in hiring, housing, healthcare, and credit — but lose authority over how it is made.

The bill pairs this freeze with real obligations for large frontier developers, defined as companies with more than $500 million in annual revenue. Those firms would face mandatory frontier AI safety frameworks, critical safety incident reporting, employee whistleblower protections, and independent audits. Crucially, it also amends the federal WARN Act to require employers to disclose when AI was a "substantial factor" in a mass layoff — an underreported labor-market provision that could matter as much as the preemption fight in the long run.

The history of this preemption drive is one of repeated overreach and tactical retreat. In July 2025, the Senate voted 99-1 to strip a 10-year moratorium on state AI laws from the Republican budget reconciliation package — the "One Big Beautiful Bill." That near-unanimous rejection was delivered by a bipartisan coalition including Senator Marsha Blackburn (R-TN), who worried the moratorium would gut children's online safety protections. When House GOP leaders tried again in November 2025 to insert a preemption provision into the National Defense Authorization Act, it again collapsed against Republican defections, with Senator Josh Hawley calling it "a terrible provision." President Trump, who has repeatedly called for "one federal standard," was left threatening executive action but delivering only a scaled-back voluntary cybersecurity order.

The Obernolte-Trahan draft is a deliberate narrowing of prior attempts — three years, not ten; development only, not deployment. But it is drawing the same critics. The House Commission on AI and the Innovation Economy, co-chaired by Representative Ted Lieu (D-CA), declared the draft "cannot serve as the basis for productive dialogue." Public Citizen, Public Knowledge, and the AFL-CIO have all registered opposition. Brad Carson of Americans for Responsible Innovation called preemption a "generational mistake." More awkwardly for the bill's prospects, Anthropic — a company that would benefit from a single national framework — has argued that any preemption should be "construed narrowly, with ambiguity resolved in favor of preserving state authority."

The competing perspectives divide along principled, not merely partisan, lines. Supporters argue that AI models are trained in one state and deployed across all fifty — what Obernolte and Trahan called "risks that don't stop at state lines" — and that a patchwork of 50 development standards would be unworkable and could fragment the US market at precisely the moment China is pressing its own AI ambitions. Critics counter that the bill transforms a potential federal floor of protections into a ceiling: by suspending state development laws before robust federal equivalents are fully tested and enforced, it hands the industry a regulatory holiday with a three-year fuse. The Washington Legal Foundation, in analysis published this week, noted that even the three-year narrowing may not survive the same Republican fault lines that doomed earlier efforts, given that Sen. Blackburn and others remain uneasy about anything that limits states' ability to protect children and consumers.

The Colorado subplot illuminates the underlying dynamics. Colorado's original comprehensive AI law — the first in the nation — has been gutted and rewritten. In May 2026, Governor Jared Polis signed SB 26-189, which repeals the original statute and replaces it with a narrower automated-decision transparency framework effective January 1, 2027. The original law's risk-management requirements, duty of care, and impact assessments are gone. Industry pressure, a DOJ legal challenge joined alongside an xAI lawsuit, and pro-innovation sentiment created a political environment in which even Colorado's Democratic-controlled legislature blinked. The state's tortured path through repeal-and-replace is precisely the outcome that preemption advocates point to as evidence that state-level AI regulation is converging toward the industry's preferences anyway — without federal action needed. Critics see it differently: Colorado capitulated under political and litigation pressure, and that capitulation should be a warning, not a template.

Congress breaks for recess in August. The Obernolte-Trahan draft must survive committee markup, a Senate companion, and a White House that has shown it will issue executive orders when Congress stalls. Whether the Great American AI Act represents a genuine federal floor or an industry-backed ceiling will likely be determined not by its text but by the amendments it survives — and the ones it doesn't.
