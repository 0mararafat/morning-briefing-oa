HIGH AI Regulation & Governance

Great American AI Act: First Federal AI Framework Enters Public Comment

The bipartisan 269-page GAAIA draft — proposing a 3-year preemption of state AI laws and binding safety rules on frontier labs — is the most significant US AI governance moment since the Biden-era executive order.
▾

On June 4, Representatives Jay Obernolte (R-CA) and Lori Trahan (D-MA) released a 269-page discussion draft of the Great American Artificial Intelligence Act of 2026, the most comprehensive federal AI governance proposal ever put forward by Congress. The bill is currently in public comment phase before formal introduction. It covers four major titles: Frontier AI Governance; Workforce; Cybersecurity; and Research, Development, and International Cooperation.

The headline provision is a three-year preemption of state laws related to AI development — effectively freezing Colorado's AI Act (scheduled to take effect June 30), California's various AI bills, and all other state-level AI regulation on model development for three years. Critically, states would retain the ability to regulate the use of AI systems within their borders, but would lose the ability to legislate on how those systems are built. Critics, including progressive state attorneys general, say this strips states of their role as a backstop for tech accountability.

For frontier AI companies with over $500 million in annual revenue, the bill would impose binding requirements: publishing public Frontier AI Frameworks, reporting critical safety incidents to the federal government, and creating whistleblower protections for employees. It also aligns with Trump's June 2 executive order establishing a voluntary 30-day pre-release review of frontier models by the NSA. Bloomberg Government and Roll Call are tracking stakeholder feedback closely. The deadline for the Colorado AI Act — June 30 — creates an immediate political pressure point if the GAAIA is not fast-tracked.

The GAAIA Preemption Trap: Colorado June 30 Deadline Forces Fed-State Battle Over AI Liability

Congress's three-year preemption of state AI development laws sounds federal-first, but it leaves state deployment regulation intact—triggering a liability vacuum where neither level of government owns frontier model safety outcomes.
AI Regulation & GovernanceUS Policy & PowerFrontier Tech / AI
▾

The Great American AI Act's headline preemption is seductive for venture capital: freeze Colorado, California, and every state AI development bill for three years, let frontier labs move fast without 50 fragmented rule sets. But the actual structure is a nightmare for both safety and liability. The GAAIA preserves state authority over AI deployment and use, while stripping it from development. This creates a regulatory void: frontier labs like Anthropic build models under federal frameworks (NSA 30-day review, binding safety incident reporting), but the moment those models hit consumer or worker-facing deployment in California or New York, suddenly state law applies. Except the states no longer control the model's safety architecture—only how it's applied. When a Claude instance trained under federal oversight causes harm in someone's workplace in California, who owns the safety failure? The lab says 'we met federal requirements.' California says 'you're deploying in our jurisdiction.' No one owns it. The June 30 Colorado AI Act enforcement deadline is the fuse. If GAAIA doesn't pass by then, Colorado becomes the first state to enforce AI development standards on a frontier lab, setting a precedent for California pileup. Congress is gambling it can jam this through before the collision. The rollout suggests they will fail: public comment period, then formal introduction, then committee, then floor. That is a September-October timeline minimum. Colorado hits first. Watch for Anthropic and Microsoft to preemptively challenge Colorado AI Act in federal court on June 28-29, claiming the MOU framework for federal preemption already exists via the executive order, making state law redundant. This becomes the Affordable Care Act moment for tech regulation.

The play reveals Apple's real strategy: outsource frontier capability, own on-device inference and encryption, control the user experience layer, and avoid the capex and talent war for frontier labs. TODAY'S DEEP DIVE

The G7's AI Summit Is Happening Right Now — and It May Bury Global Governance

As the world's three most powerful AI lab chiefs sit down with G7 leaders in Évian today, the US is quietly ensuring that any joint commitments on AI regulation are watered down to nothing — revealing a fracture in the Western alliance that China will exploit.
The 52nd G7 Summit is live today in Évian-les-Bains — with the CEOs of OpenAI, Google DeepMind, and Anthropic all present — and the outcome of Wednesday's dedicated AI working lunch will determine whether the G7 produces meaningful governance commitments or formally ratifies the US preference for voluntary-only AI standards.

The 52nd G7 Summit is underway in Évian-les-Bains on the French shore of Lake Geneva, running through Wednesday, June 17. It is, by most accounts, arriving at one of the most volatile moments in the post-Cold War order: a potential US-Iran peace deal still in flux, European fury over US tariffs, and a NATO alliance whose coherence is openly in doubt. But threaded through all of it, less visibly, is a fight over who gets to write the rules of artificial intelligence — and whether those rules exist at all.

For the first time in any G7 gathering, the chief executives of the three dominant AI laboratories are present at the same summit. Sam Altman of OpenAI attended at the personal invitation of French President Emmanuel Macron. Google DeepMind's Demis Hassabis and Anthropic's Dario Amodei confirmed attendance shortly afterward. Also on the guest list: Mistral AI's Arthur Mensch, Cohere's Aidan Gomez, Salesforce's Marc Benioff, and a constellation of European and Asian AI founders. The Élysée arranged a dedicated working lunch on Wednesday for political leaders and technology executives to discuss AI deployment, infrastructure, and regulatory questions. The symbolism is unmistakable: France is staging the moment as a founding document for allied AI governance.

The problem is that the document may come out nearly blank.

Analysts tracking pre-summit negotiations report that the United States has signaled firm resistance to multilateral AI governance commitments, and that any references to AI regulation in the G7 leaders' communiqué are expected to be deliberately watered down. Where two years ago the G7's Hiroshima AI Process produced guiding principles and a voluntary code of conduct for AI developers, the 2026 edition is likely to emphasize AI's economic benefits and strip out governance language that Washington fears could constrain American industrial advantage. The Hiroshima process itself — running since Japan's 2023 presidency — has so far yielded principles and codes of conduct but no enforceable regulation. Évian was supposed to be the moment it sharpened. Instead, it may mark the moment the effort goes soft.

The US position is not merely defensive. Washington has an affirmative AI agenda: the "American AI technology stack." An executive order signed in July 2025, and a Commerce Department call for proposals that closed June 30, set up the American AI Exports Program — a government-coordinated push to export bundled US semiconductors, cloud infrastructure, AI models, and technical standards as a single package to allied and swing-state nations. The explicit goal is to crowd out Chinese AI infrastructure globally. That agenda requires maximum flexibility for US firms and maximum suspicion of multilateral governance that could constrain them. Voluntary commitments that OpenAI's chief global affairs officer expects to emerge from Évian — focused narrowly on youth safety and frontier cyber risks — are not incidental; they are the ceiling the US is willing to accept.

European leaders arrive with reasons to push harder. The EU's Tech Sovereignty Package, announced June 3, signals a Brussels that has decided it cannot afford to source more than 80 percent of its digital infrastructure externally. France under Macron is pursuing a contradictory strategy simultaneously: positioning France as a pro-AI, pro-growth hub to attract investment — hence Altman's invitation — while also wanting enforceable governance frameworks that would apply to US firms operating in Europe. German and Italian officials, meanwhile, are already managing the political fallout from US tariffs and NATO ambiguity, making them reluctant to open a second front over AI governance at precisely the moment they need Washington's goodwill.

Canadian Prime Minister Mark Carney arrived in France having just told Trinity College Dublin that "the post-Cold War world's rules-based order is breaking down." That framing, stark as it is, captures the real stakes beneath the AI debate: this is not simply a technical disagreement about model auditing standards. It is a contest over whether the G7 remains a forum for rule-making among democratic allies, or a photo backdrop for competing national AI strategies dressed up as cooperation.

The competition perspectives are genuine. Optimists argue that voluntary commitments, even thin ones, create institutional precedents that can be strengthened over time; the Hiroshima code of conduct became a reference point for the EU AI Act's international provisions. Pessimists note that the window for meaningful pre-deployment governance is closing rapidly: frontier models are now releasing at a cadence measured in weeks, and the models Altman, Amodei, and Hassabis are currently shipping — including systems that have drawn government scrutiny for cyber and biological risk potential — are already deployed before any G7 framework could reach them.

The downstream effects of a weak Évian communiqué will be felt beyond the summit itself. A parallel G7 data protection authority roundtable is scheduled in Paris on June 23, where national regulators are attempting to align AI enforcement as the EU AI Act's high-risk system deadline approaches. Analysts note that if the Évian leaders' communiqué produces a divided or weakened AI governance signal, national regulators will have diminished political cover to pursue ambitious cross-border coordination — leaving enforcement patchwork exactly as the most capable AI systems reach mass deployment.

Watch for the communiqué language on AI to emerge Wednesday evening Paris time, the specific framing of any "voluntary commitments" signed by the tech executives present, and whether the G7 DPA roundtable on June 23 proceeds with ambition or retreats to minimalism in Évian's wake. The trajectory of allied AI governance for the next two years may be set in the next forty-eight hours.
