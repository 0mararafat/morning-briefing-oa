Watch: whether the June 8 exchange of strikes triggers full re-escalation or whether Pakistan-mediated talks produce a durable agreement before the SpaceX IPO and FOMC meeting this week, both of which would be acutely sensitive to any energy price shock.

HIGH AI Regulation & Governance

Congress Drops Great American AI Act: 3-Year State Preemption, Frontier Lab Audits

A bipartisan 269-page House bill would freeze state AI development laws for three years and impose mandatory six-month safety audits on frontier labs—the most substantive federal AI governance proposal yet.
▾

On June 4, Reps. Jay Obernolte (R-CA) and Lori Trahan (D-MA) released a discussion draft of the Great American Artificial Intelligence Act of 2026, a 269-page bipartisan proposal that would establish the first comprehensive federal AI framework. The bill's most contested provision would preempt state laws specifically regulating AI model development for three years, though states would retain authority over AI deployment. It targets 'large frontier developers'—companies with over $500 million in annual revenue—requiring mandatory independent audits every six months, public pre-release risk disclosures, and civil penalties of up to $1 million per violation per day for non-compliance. The bill formally defines 'catastrophic risk' as a foreseeable threat of death or injury to more than 50 people or $1 billion in property damage from WMD development, cyberattack, or loss of meaningful human oversight. It would establish the Center for AI Standards and Innovation (CAISI) under the Commerce Department with $300 million over three years. The bill arrived just two days after Trump signed his voluntary frontier model access executive order, creating a layered and somewhat contradictory federal posture. Reaction was sharply divided: tech industry association ITI praised bipartisan leadership, while AFL-CIO, the AFT, Public Citizen, and safety groups issued strongly negative responses. Public Citizen called it 'a disastrous proposal that Big Tech is celebrating,' arguing it strips states of accountability tools while offering no guarantee of federal safeguards. Roll Call noted Colorado's June 30 AI Act enforcement deadline and California's active state regime add urgency to the preemption debate. This is currently a discussion draft soliciting public feedback before formal introduction.

The Great American AI Act is nominally bipartisan, but it's actually a regulatory arbitrage play that weakens Anthropic's competitive position right before its IPO. That's probably not intentional. But it's the structural outcome.

TODAY'S DEEP DIVE

SpaceX Goes Public as an AI Infrastructure Giant — but the Governance Story Is as Big as the Valuation

Three days before the largest IPO in history, SpaceX has locked in $26 billion in annualized AI compute revenue from Google and Anthropic — two rivals of its own AI division — while Morningstar calls its $1.75 trillion valuation a "material threat of value destruction."
SpaceX lists on Nasdaq on June 12 — three days from now — and this week's flood of SEC disclosures, governance critiques, and Alphabet's record $84.75 billion equity raise have crystallized every major tension in the deal simultaneously.

On June 12, SpaceX is scheduled to list on Nasdaq under the ticker SPCX at $135 per share, targeting a $75 billion raise and a valuation of approximately $1.75 trillion — a figure that would make it the largest public offering in history, surpassing Brazil's Petrobras $70 billion sale in 2010. But the story that has dominated markets this week is not rockets: it is compute.

On June 5, an SEC filing revealed that Google had agreed to pay SpaceX $920 million per month from October 2026 through June 2029 — roughly $30 billion in total — for access to approximately 110,000 Nvidia GPUs housed in SpaceX's data center infrastructure. This followed an earlier, even larger deal disclosed in May: Anthropic agreed to pay $1.25 billion per month through 2029 to rent the entire output of SpaceX's Colossus 1 data center in Memphis, covering more than 220,000 GPUs drawing 300 megawatts of power. Together, the two contracts represent roughly $26 billion in annualized contracted revenue, transforming what analysts had called xAI's biggest financial liability — a $2.5 billion operating loss in Q1 2026 alone — into a predictable revenue base heading into the roadshow.

The background matters enormously. In February 2026, Musk merged his AI company xAI into SpaceX in an all-stock transaction that was, at the time, the largest private-sector M&A deal in history, creating a combined entity then valued at $1.25 trillion. The strategic logic was that xAI would power SpaceX's ambitious orbital AI compute ambitions — Musk has spoken publicly of launching up to a million satellites to run solar-powered data centers in orbit, a concept now formally embedded in SpaceX's SEC filings under the term "AI compute satellite." The reality on the ground was less flattering: xAI had burned through $12.7 billion in capital expenditure in 2025 and was losing corporate clients to Claude, Gemini, and ChatGPT. The Colossus GPU cluster, built at enormous cost, sat with excess capacity. The Anthropic and Google deals effectively turned that surplus into a cash machine — but at the cost of renting Musk's AI infrastructure to the very companies Grok is supposed to beat.

For Google, the deal reveals a different kind of desperation. Alphabet raised $84.75 billion in equity — the largest equity offering ever recorded — between June 1 and June 2, with Berkshire Hathaway committing $10 billion as anchor investor. CEO Sundar Pichai has told analysts explicitly that Google Cloud "would have been higher if we were able to meet the demand," and the company's full-year 2026 capex guidance of $180 billion to $190 billion is nearly double what it spent in all of 2025. The SpaceX deal, which Google described internally as "bridge capacity" for its Gemini Enterprise agent platform, is a public admission that the world's most valuable advertising and cloud company cannot build fast enough to serve its own customers.

The competing interpretations here are sharp. Bulls see a convergence of clean signals: SpaceX has multi-year contracted revenue with two of the best-funded AI companies in the world, a Starlink satellite internet business generating strong cash flow, and a monopoly on the most reliable heavy-lift rocket infrastructure on the planet. The $75 billion in future contracted AI revenue nearly matches the $75 billion the company is raising in its IPO — an unusual and compelling symmetry.

Bears and governance specialists see something darker. Morningstar's debut coverage of SpaceX placed fair value at $780 billion — less than half the IPO price — flagging the AI segment's $6.36 billion operating loss in 2025 and calling the xAI merger a "material threat of value destruction." The merger itself was conducted without the standard protections that public markets demand: no independent board committee, no minority shareholder vote, no arm's-length fairness review. Musk both negotiated and approved a transaction that transferred his own company into another company he controls. A Danish pension fund has already placed SpaceX on its investment blacklist, citing "catastrophic" governance. Musk will hold approximately 85% voting control through a dual-class share structure, meaning new public shareholders will have virtually no ability to challenge decisions he makes.

The 90-day bilateral termination clauses in both the Anthropic and Google contracts add a further layer of fragility. Industry-standard cloud contracts run five to ten years; these run through 2029 in name but can be unwound in a quarter. Google holds roughly 5% of SpaceX, meaning it has a direct financial interest in a high IPO valuation — a fact that makes the $920 million monthly deal harder to read as a purely arm's-length transaction. Bank of America strategist Michael Hartnett has separately warned that SpaceX, OpenAI, and Anthropic all landing in public markets this quarter could push tech concentration in the S&P 500 past the 48% threshold that has historically preceded market dislocations.

Watch for the following on a short timeline: First-day trading on June 12 will set the psychological anchor for a valuation the market has never had to price before. Index inclusion decisions by Morningstar, MSCI, and S&P — being fast-tracked given the company's size — will determine whether trillions in passive capital are forced to buy SPCX regardless of conviction. The first insider sell window opens with second-quarter earnings, likely in August, and that is where real price discovery begins. Whether Google and Anthropic exercise their 90-day termination options after December 31 will reveal how durable the compute landlord model actually is — or whether it was primarily engineered to close the most ambitious IPO of the decade.
